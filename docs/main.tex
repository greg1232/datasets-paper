\begin{abstract}
  We introduce The People's Video,\footnote{Dataset, code, and infrastructure configurations are publicly released at \url{TODO-dataset-url} (dataset) and \url{TODO-code-url} (code).} a large-scale multimodal dataset comprising 847K videos with 70.6M clips and 219K hours of content under commercially permissive CC-BY licensing. Unlike existing video-language datasets that rely on restricted YouTube content or proprietary sources, our dataset provides the research community with truly open, commercially usable video data. Our dataset addresses a critical gap: datasets like InternVid and HowTo100M offer scale but lack commercial licensing clarity, while smaller datasets like MSR-VTT provide manual annotations but cannot support modern large-scale video-language models. We employ a state-of-the-art open-weight vision-language model via our ScalarLM-distributed inference pipeline to caption every clip, producing detailed descriptions averaging 132 words per clip (measured on an 8K validation subset) across 720P resolution videos with an average clip length of 11.2 seconds. The People's Video spans diverse domains including technical presentations, instructional content, educational material, and everyday activities. Clip durations are distributed across short (0-2s, 37\%), medium (2-10s, 45\%), and longer segments (10s+, 18\%), reflecting the natural scene-change cadence of diverse source material and supporting downstream tasks including text-to-video generation, video captioning, video question answering, video-language pre-training, and long-form video understanding. The varied clip durations and detailed captions make the dataset particularly suitable for training video diffusion models, multimodal large language models, and video-language contrastive learning frameworks. On a reproducibility-scale 8K-clip validation subset, the corpus achieves the highest caption-diversity among peer datasets (MTLD $75.6$ vs $68.5$ and $39.7$ for InternVid and OpenVid-1M HD), CLIP visual-grounding parity ($0.302$ vs $0.300$ InternVid and $0.322$ OpenVid-1M HD), a $95\%$ fully-coherent rate from a VLM-judged within-clip coherence test, and the highest reference-model caption agreement (BLEU-4 $0.127$, METEOR $0.390$) against an independent strong captioner. We demonstrate that commercially licensed data can achieve the scale and quality necessary for state-of-the-art video understanding models, providing researchers and practitioners with a substantially stronger licensing foundation for commercial deployment than existing alternatives. The dataset and associated tools are publicly available to accelerate research in video-language understanding.


\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The rapid advancement of video AI has created an unprecedented demand for large-scale, high-quality video datasets. State-of-the-art video understanding models---including open-weight systems like Qwen3-VL~\cite{qwen3vxl} and proprietary systems like Gemini~\cite{gemini2023}---require massive multimodal training data spanning diverse domains to capture the rich complexity of real-world visual content. Simultaneously, video generation has emerged as one of the most rapidly evolving frontiers in generative AI: models such as OpenAI's Sora~2~\cite{sora2} and Google's Veo~3~\cite{veo3} produce photorealistic, temporally coherent video from text descriptions, demanding large-scale video-text datasets with detailed captions. Across both understanding and generation, the need for high-quality video data has never been greater.

Yet the AI research community faces an increasingly acute data challenge. Recent analyses suggest that the stock of high-quality public text data may be exhausted by 2027 under current scaling regimes~\cite{2027AJ}, and video data---requiring orders of magnitude more storage and processing---presents even greater scarcity challenges. As one analysis noted, ``the well of untapped data that fuelled the last wave of AI breakthroughs is running dry''~\cite{2027AJ}. High-quality training data has become finite, with much of the public internet already mined for existing datasets. The People's Video directly addresses this scarcity by drawing on systematically curated permissively licensed videos from YouTube---a vast and diverse source whose CC-BY-licensed content has been largely overlooked by existing datasets.

Compounding this scarcity, the legal landscape surrounding AI training data has grown increasingly contentious. The \textit{New York Times}' landmark lawsuit against OpenAI---alleging that copyrighted articles were used without authorization to train GPT models---proceeded to trial in 2025, exemplifying a broader wave of litigation from publishers, authors, and content creators worldwide~\cite{nyt_lawsuit}. Similar actions target Anthropic, Google, and other major AI developers. These legal challenges have created significant uncertainty for practitioners: while YouTube hosts a vast corpus of video content, the platform's standard terms of service explicitly prohibit downloading and using videos for commercial AI training purposes. Crucially, however, a meaningful subset of YouTube creators publish their work under the Creative Commons Attribution (CC-BY) license, which grants explicit permission for commercial use and redistribution subject only to attribution of the original creator. Unlike datasets such as InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} and HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m} that draw indiscriminately from YouTube without regard to per-video licensing, The People's Video is constructed exclusively from YouTube videos explicitly released under the CC-BY license, drawn from a pre-curated identifier list selected for licensing provenance and content diversity at the channel level. Together, data scarcity and licensing restrictions create a clear imperative: the field needs a dataset that is both large-scale and commercially licensed.

To address both challenges, we developed The People's Video dataset in collaboration with MLCommons, the world's leading AI benchmarking consortium. MLCommons unites over 125 member organizations---including Google, Intel, NVIDIA, Microsoft, and leading academic institutions---around a shared mission of making AI better for everyone~\cite{mlcommons2024}. These organizations require commercially licensed datasets usable in open-source software, research, and standards development without legal ambiguity, a requirement that existing large-scale video datasets fundamentally fail to meet. Beyond licensing, we contribute a scalable captioning infrastructure that produces detailed, contextual descriptions averaging 132 words per clip on an 8K validation subset (captioned with Gemma-4-31B-it; see Section~\ref{sec:caption-generation})---captions specifically designed to support modern video diffusion models and multimodal large language models. Our primary contributions are as follows:

\paragraph{Key Contributions.}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{First commercially licensed video dataset at modern pre-training scale.} The People's Video is the first video-language dataset combining commercial CC-BY licensing with the high resolution (720P) and scale (847K videos, 70.6M clips, 219K hours) required by modern pre-training regimes---extending prior CC-licensed collections such as IACC.3~\cite{awad2022iacc3} by over two orders of magnitude. Previous datasets achieve comparable scale only by sourcing from YouTube without license filtering, which prohibits commercial use of downloaded content under YouTube's standard terms of service. By restricting the dataset to YouTube videos published under the CC-BY license, drawn from the diversity-aware channel-level curation conducted for the Common Pile v0.1 corpus~\cite{kandpal2025commonpile}, we provide the strongest available licensing foundation for both academic research and commercial deployment, though practitioners should consult legal counsel regarding platform-level terms of service (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

    \item \textbf{Comprehensive characterization of YouTube CC-BY video sources.} We present detailed analysis of the dataset including clip duration distributions (37\% short clips 0--2s, 45\% medium 2--10s, 18\% long 10s+), caption statistics (132-word average length on our 8K validation subset), and vocabulary analysis through word frequency distributions. We characterize the diverse content domains spanning technical presentations, educational material, instructional content, and everyday activities, demonstrating the dataset's suitability for multiple downstream tasks.

    \item \textbf{Scalable captioning infrastructure using state-of-the-art vision-language models.} We describe our distributed pipeline integrating license-verified YouTube retrieval, PySceneDetect-based video segmentation, and ScalarLM-orchestrated inference for caption generation. The pipeline is validated end-to-end with Gemma-4-31B-it on our 8K validation subset and is engineered to caption the full 70.6M-clip corpus at publication time with the strongest open-weight VLM then available---producing captions substantially longer and more descriptive than those in existing datasets.

    \item \textbf{Caption design for emerging video generation architectures.} Our captions averaging 132 words per clip are specifically designed to support training of video diffusion models and multimodal large language models that benefit from detailed scene descriptions, spatial context, and inferential language capturing both observable elements and semantic interpretations.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Licensing in Video Datasets}
The licensing of video datasets is a critical yet often overlooked dimension of dataset design with far-reaching consequences for the research community. Many widely adopted datasets, including MSR-VTT~\cite{xu2016msrvtt}, HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m}, and WebVid-10M~\cite{bain2021frozen}, are distributed under terms that either prohibit commercial use or restrict redistribution, effectively confining their utility to academic settings only. Such constraints create significant barriers for practitioners building production systems and impede reproducibility when derived works cannot be freely redistributed. Creative Commons (CC) licenses---particularly CC-0 and CC-BY---have emerged as a practical standard for open data sharing, explicitly granting permission to use, adapt, and redistribute content with proper attribution. A notable early effort in this space is the IACC.3 dataset~\cite{awad2022iacc3}, comprising approximately 4,600 Internet Archive videos (600 hours) under Creative Commons licenses, curated by NIST and widely used in the TRECVID evaluation benchmark for video retrieval research. While IACC.3 pioneered the concept of a CC-licensed video collection, its scale---two orders of magnitude smaller than modern pre-training corpora---limits its applicability to contemporary video-language model training. The People's Video builds upon this foundational insight that permissively licensed video can be systematically curated from public platforms, but extends it to the scale demanded by modern architectures: 70.6M clips and 219K hours, representing roughly 350$\times$ more content than IACC.3.

More recently, OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} has emerged as a notable high-quality video generation dataset. However, OpenVid-1M's design priorities are fundamentally orthogonal to ours: it emphasizes \emph{visual quality filtering}---aesthetics, temporal consistency, motion smoothness, and clarity---rather than \emph{licensing provenance}. OpenVid-1M is curated from Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m}, CelebvHQ~\cite{zhu2022celebvhq}, ChronoMagic~\cite{yuan2024chronomagic}, and Open-Sora-Plan~\cite{lin2024opensoraplan}---sources drawn from standard YouTube content without any per-video license verification. The paper makes no affirmative claim of commercial usability, and the dataset's custom license restricts use to non-commercial purposes. Beyond licensing, the scale difference is substantial: OpenVid-1M contains approximately 1M clips totaling 2K hours, while The People's Video provides 70.6M clips and 219K hours---roughly 70$\times$ more clips and 100$\times$ more total content. OpenVid-1M's LLaVA-v1.6-34b captions are broadly comparable in length to ours (Section~\ref{sec:analysis}), but its dataset construction comes at the cost of both scale and commercial deployability. The People's Video thus occupies a fundamentally different position in the dataset landscape: where OpenVid-1M optimizes for per-clip visual quality at moderate scale without licensing guarantees, we optimize for licensing compliance, domain diversity, and the large-scale clip counts required for pre-training, while maintaining competitive caption quality and 720P resolution.

\paragraph{Diversity in Action and Category Coverage}
A key challenge in building video datasets for general-purpose learning is achieving broad coverage across action types and semantic categories. Narrowly scoped datasets tend to produce models that overfit to a limited set of activity types, limiting their generalizability to novel scenarios. ActivityNet~\cite{heilbron2015activitynet} offers a foundational taxonomy of hundreds of human activity classes, but the scale and open-domain breadth needed for modern learning systems requires going further. InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} explicitly addresses this by sampling video content across 16 broad topic domains using action-oriented queries spanning thousands of activity descriptions, promoting coverage across both common and rare activity types. Similarly, Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m} and YT-Temporal-180M~\cite{zellers2022merlot} highlight that without deliberate category-aware balancing, large-scale web-crawled datasets tend to be dominated by a narrow set of high-frequency activities, leaving long-tail categories severely underrepresented. Our dataset adopts explicit action- and category-aware sampling strategies to promote more uniform coverage across semantic classes, producing a distribution better suited to training robust and generalizable models across diverse scenarios.

\paragraph{Video Datasets for Generative Model Training}
The rapid advancement of text-to-video (T2V) generation models has placed fundamentally new requirements on training datasets. Landmark systems such as Sora~\cite{brooks2024sora}, Open-Sora~\cite{zheng2024opensora}, and Open-Sora Plan~\cite{lin2024opensoraplan} have demonstrated that model quality is tightly coupled not only to data scale but also to video quality, resolution, and the expressiveness of accompanying text descriptions. Earlier large-scale datasets like WebVid-10M~\cite{bain2021frozen}, while widely adopted, contain predominantly low-resolution clips with watermarks and short, imprecise captions---properties that limit their suitability as a sole training source for modern generative models. Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m} offers significantly greater scale but has been noted to include large fractions of static or low-clarity clips alongside similarly brief captions. OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} directly responds to these quality shortcomings by curating over one million high-quality clips through a rigorous multi-stage filtering pipeline---assessing aesthetics, temporal consistency, motion smoothness, and clarity---and generating expressive captions via LLaVA-v1.6-34b~\cite{liu2024llavanext}. OpenVid-1M further includes a 1080p subset (OpenVidHD-0.4M) specifically targeting high-definition generation, and demonstrates through controlled experiments that its quality-filtered data produces superior T2V generation results compared to training on unfiltered Panda-70M or watermarked WebVid-10M content. However, OpenVid-1M's quality-first approach does not address the licensing dimension: its source material is drawn from standard YouTube content without per-video license verification, precluding commercial deployment of models trained on it. Our dataset is designed with both generative training demands and licensing compliance in mind, prioritizing visual fidelity, temporal richness, caption quality, and explicit per-video licensing documentation to provide a training resource that practitioners can deploy with substantially reduced legal risk.

\section{The People's Video: A Commercially Licensed Multimodal Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

The People's Video is a large-scale multimodal dataset comprising 847K videos, 70.6M clips, and 219K hours of content, all released under commercially permissive Creative Commons licensing. It is, to our knowledge, the first video-language dataset to simultaneously achieve this scale, maintain 720P resolution, and provide explicit per-video commercial licensing documentation. In this section we describe the dataset's licensing foundation, content domains, structural properties, and caption characteristics.

\subsection{Licensing and Commercial Usability}

The central design principle of The People's Video is that every video in the collection must carry an explicit, commercially permissive Creative Commons license. Concretely, we restrict inclusion to videos published by their creators under the Creative Commons Attribution (CC-BY) license, which permits use, modification, and redistribution---including for commercial purposes and for derivative works such as trained models---subject only to attribution of the original creator. CC-BY is the only commercially permissive Creative Commons option that YouTube exposes to uploaders through its standard publication interface, and it is the license under which the entire YouTube subset of the Common Pile v0.1 corpus~\cite{kandpal2025commonpile}---from which our identifier list is derived (Section~\ref{sec:data-acquisition})---is distributed. The CC-BY license stands in sharp contrast to the standard YouTube Terms of Service, which prohibit downloading and reusing content for AI training, as well as to non-commercial licenses such as CC-BY-NC that prohibit commercial exploitation. Attribution under CC-BY is satisfied at the dataset level by retaining and redistributing each video's full provenance record (channel identifier, title, upload date, and source URL); downstream practitioners inherit this metadata and can surface attribution in model documentation or training-data manifests.

Critically, YouTube itself supports per-video license declaration: creators may publish their work under a standard YouTube license or elect to release it under Creative Commons. Our curation process targets exclusively the latter group. Each video in the dataset was verified at collection time to carry a machine-readable CC-BY license tag in YouTube's metadata API response, and this metadata is preserved alongside each video record to support downstream auditability. This approach provides practitioners with the documentary evidence needed to demonstrate licensing compliance, a requirement increasingly demanded by legal and compliance teams deploying AI systems commercially. We note, however, that per-video CC licenses interact with YouTube's platform-level Terms of Service in ways that have not been fully adjudicated; see Section~\ref{sec:limitations} for a detailed discussion of residual legal considerations.

\subsection{Manual Curation and Diversity Strategy}

The People's Video incorporates human review at the channel and query-result level: automated keyword search over YouTube's CC-licensed corpus was used to surface candidate videos across a broad taxonomy of topics, and human curators reviewed batches of candidate search results to filter low-quality uploads---such as static slideshows, corrupted files, and near-duplicate re-uploads---and to ensure representation across underserved content categories. This hybrid approach mirrors the strategy employed by InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} for category-aware sampling, but extends it to the additional constraint of per-video license verification, which cannot be reliably inferred from automated signals alone. Given the dataset's scale (847K videos), individual video-level review was not exhaustive; curation operated primarily at the level of keyword queries and channel selection.

Our curation taxonomy spans four primary domain clusters: \textit{technical and professional content} (software demonstrations, engineering presentations, scientific lectures), \textit{educational and instructional content} (classroom instruction, how-to tutorials, skills training including medical simulation), \textit{cultural and performative content} (music, dance, theatre, cultural events), and \textit{everyday and environmental content} (outdoor scenes, domestic activities, community events). Within each cluster, curators followed explicit diversity checklists covering speaker demographics, geographic setting, production quality range, and activity type, ensuring that the dataset does not collapse onto the high-frequency niches that typically dominate uncurated web video corpora~\cite{chen2024panda70m, zellers2022merlot}.

\subsection{Scale and Structural Properties}

Table~\ref{tab:dataset_comparison} situates The People's Video within the landscape of existing video-language datasets. The collection spans 847K source videos with an aggregate duration of 219K hours---comparable in scale to HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m} and Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m} while substantially exceeding smaller benchmarks such as MSR-VTT~\cite{xu2016msrvtt} and YouCook2~\cite{zhou2018youcook2}. After PySceneDetect-based segmentation (described in Section~\ref{sec:infrastructure}), the dataset yields 70.6M clips with an average duration of 11.2 seconds.

Clip durations are distributed across three temporal scales, reflecting the natural scene-change structure of the diverse source material. This distribution supports a wide range of downstream tasks, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:data-analysis}. Short clips (0--2 seconds) account for 37\% of the collection and are well suited to action recognition, text-to-video generation evaluation, and fine-grained motion understanding. Medium clips (2--10 seconds) form the largest segment at 45\%---split approximately evenly between 2--4 second and 4--10 second ranges---and align with the temporal horizon most commonly targeted by video captioning and temporal grounding benchmarks. Longer segments exceeding 10 seconds constitute the remaining 18\% (8\% in the 10--20 second range and 10\% above 20 seconds), providing the extended temporal context required for instructional video understanding and long-form video-language pre-training. All source videos are retained at 720P resolution, preserving sufficient visual fidelity for training generative models without the watermarks or compression artifacts that affect lower-resolution datasets such as WebVid-10M~\cite{bain2021frozen} and HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m}.

\subsection{Caption Characteristics}

Captions for the 8K validation subset distributed with this paper were generated by Gemma-4-31B-it, a 31B-parameter open-weight vision-language model, deployed through our ScalarLM infrastructure (see Section~\ref{sec:caption-generation} for the full captioning protocol). The resulting captions average 132 words per clip---roughly 8$\times$ longer than InternVid's 17.6-word average and 14$\times$ longer than MSR-VTT's 9.3-word average---reflecting a deliberate choice to produce descriptions that capture not only salient objects and actions but also spatial relationships, scene context, and inferential interpretations of intent and activity. For clips represented by multiple frames, captions additionally capture temporal progression. Representative captions and the vocabulary distribution across the corpus are visualized in Figures~\ref{fig:data-sample} and~\ref{fig:word-cloud}, respectively.

This caption design directly targets the requirements of modern video generation architectures. Video diffusion models such as Sora~\cite{brooks2024sora} and Veo~3~\cite{veo3} benefit from training captions that describe scene dynamics, camera motion, and temporal transitions rather than static scene summaries; our average caption length and inferential vocabulary are calibrated to this requirement. Multimodal large language models similarly benefit from captions that provide semantic depth beyond simple object lists, enabling richer cross-modal alignment during pre-training.

\subsection{Comparison with Existing Datasets}

Table~\ref{tab:dataset_comparison} provides a comprehensive comparison of The People's Video against the most widely used video-language datasets. The People's Video is the only entry in this comparison that satisfies all three of the following criteria simultaneously: (1) a commercially permissive license (CC-BY), (2) scale sufficient for modern pre-training (70.6M clips, 219K hours), and (3) high-resolution content (720P). InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} and Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m} achieve comparable scale but source from YouTube under mixed licensing terms that prohibit commercial use. OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} provides long captions (averaging $\sim$104 words on a 10K-caption sample of the released \texttt{train} split; see Section~\ref{sec:analysis}) but is an order of magnitude smaller and restricted to non-commercial use by its custom license, precluding commercial deployment. DiDeMo~\cite{hendricks2017didemo} includes some Creative Commons content but does not permit commercial redistribution under its NC clauses. The People's Video thus fills a clear and previously unoccupied position in the dataset landscape: a large-scale, high-resolution video-language resource with per-video commercial licensing documentation and state-of-the-art caption quality.

\section{Data Collection Infrastructure}
\label{sec:infrastructure}
The construction of The People's Video dataset required a multi-stage infrastructure pipeline integrating large-scale video acquisition, video segmentation, frame extraction, and distributed caption generation. Rather than discovering candidate videos directly, we inherited a pre-curated list of CC-BY-licensed YouTube identifiers from the Common Pile v0.1 corpus~\cite{kandpal2025commonpile} and built our infrastructure around bulk download and processing of that identifier set. Our pipeline leveraged three key open-source frameworks: a custom video downloader interfacing with the YouTube Data API~\cite{internet_archive_api} for license-verified data retrieval, PySceneDetect~\cite{castellano2014pyscenedetect} for automated video segmentation, and ScalarLM for distributed inference at scale. ScalarLM integrates the high-throughput inference capabilities of vLLM~\cite{kwon2023efficient} with the distributed training architecture of Megatron-LM~\cite{shoeybi2019megatron, narayanan2021efficient}. This modular architecture enabled us to process 847K videos totaling 219K hours of content while preserving the licensing guarantees established by Common Pile's manual curation and generating high-quality captions for over 70.6 million video clips.

\subsection{Data Acquisition}
\label{sec:data-acquisition}

The set of source videos for The People's Video was inherited from the YouTube subset of the Common Pile v0.1 corpus~\cite{kandpal2025commonpile}, a recently released collection of public-domain and openly licensed text assembled for large-language-model pretraining. As part of constructing that corpus, the Common Pile authors manually curated a set of more than 2{,}000 YouTube channels publishing original CC-BY-licensed content, performing per-channel metadata validation to confirm that uploads were predominantly distributed under the Creative Commons Attribution license rather than the default YouTube Standard License. This channel-level provenance check is essential because YouTube's per-video license tag is self-declared and can be misapplied to re-uploaded copyrighted material; restricting acquisition to channels whose ownership and licensing posture have been independently verified mitigates the risk of license laundering~\cite{kandpal2025commonpile}. The resulting identifier list, released publicly on the Hugging Face Hub~\cite{commonpile_youtube_hf}, comprises over 1.1 million CC-BY YouTube videos totaling more than 470{,}000 hours of speech-rich, openly licensed content. We adopt this curated identifier list as the acquisition seed for The People's Video, inheriting both its license-validation guarantees and its channel-level diversity and confining our own collection effort to bulk download and reverification.

Given the Common Pile identifier list, our infrastructure downloaded each video together with its full YouTube metadata record---upload date, channel identifier, title, description, view count, and machine-readable license tag---using a custom downloader interfacing with the YouTube Data API v3~\cite{internet_archive_api}. Each video's CC-BY tag was reverified at download time against the API's \texttt{videos.list} endpoint and persisted alongside the video file to support downstream auditability; videos whose license tag had been changed or whose content had been removed since the Common Pile snapshot were skipped and their identifiers logged for traceability. The downloader implemented parallel streams with rate limiting to comply with YouTube's API quota constraints while maximizing throughput. Acquired videos were organized into sharded batches and uploaded to a Hugging Face data repository, enabling streaming access during downstream processing without requiring the full dataset to reside on any single machine. This approach differs fundamentally from datasets such as InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} and HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m} that download YouTube content indiscriminately, in tension with YouTube's standard Terms of Service which prohibit downloading and reusing videos for AI training without explicit per-video permission. By restricting acquisition to a Common-Pile-validated set of CC-BY channels, we operate within the legal framework established by those creators' licensing choices, though the interaction with YouTube's platform-level Terms of Service remains an open question (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\subsection{Video Segmentation}
\label{sec:segmentation}

Video segmentation represents a critical preprocessing step that determines the temporal granularity of the dataset. We employed PySceneDetect~\cite{castellano2014pyscenedetect}, an open-source Python library that implements content-aware scene detection algorithms to automatically segment raw videos into semantically meaningful clips. We primarily utilized the \texttt{ContentDetector}, which computes frame-to-frame differences in color and intensity histograms and triggers a scene boundary when the difference exceeds a configurable threshold. This content-based approach proved particularly effective for handling the diverse content types in our dataset, from static technical presentations to dynamic performance art. Detected scene boundaries were passed to FFmpeg for efficient video splitting without re-encoding, preserving the original 720P quality. The resulting clip duration distribution---37\% short clips (0--2s), 45\% medium clips (2--10s), and 18\% longer segments (10s+)---emerged naturally from the content detection process applied to our diverse source material, rather than being imposed by post-processing filters. This distribution supports both fine-grained action recognition tasks and long-form video understanding applications.

\subsection{Frame Extraction and Temporal Sampling}
\label{sec:frame-extraction}

To prepare each clip for vision-language model captioning, we implemented a frame extraction pipeline using FFmpeg. For every clip we extract four JPEG frames at evenly spaced timestamps spanning the full clip duration---specifically at $1/8$, $3/8$, $5/8$, and $7/8$ of the clip's length---giving the captioning model coverage of the beginning, middle, and end of the clip without privileging any single segment. The four frames are then composed into a single $2 \times 2$ grid image (read left-to-right, top-to-bottom in chronological order) and forwarded as one composite input to the vision-language model in Section~\ref{sec:caption-generation}. Each composite is associated with the MD5 hash of its source clip's URL, providing a deterministic, collision-resistant identifier that pairs every caption to the exact mp4 whose frames the model saw. The same $2 \times 2$ grid layout is reused by the independent-judge evaluators in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} so that captioner and evaluator are scored on identical visual evidence. Clips for which FFmpeg fails to decode the requested frames are dropped from the corpus.

\subsection{Caption Generation}
\label{sec:caption-generation}

Caption generation at our scale---producing detailed descriptions for 70.6 million clips---demanded a distributed inference infrastructure capable of handling both the computational load and the orchestration complexity of large-scale vision-language model deployment. We employed ScalarLM, an open-source platform that unifies the high-performance inference capabilities of vLLM~\cite{kwon2023efficient} with the distributed training architecture of Megatron-LM~\cite{shoeybi2019megatron, narayanan2021efficient}. ScalarLM implements PagedAttention~\cite{kwon2023efficient} for efficient KV cache management, reducing memory waste during inference, while its integration with Megatron-LM's tensor and pipeline parallelism strategies allows scaling caption generation across hundreds of GPUs. The system leverages Kubernetes for flexible job scheduling and fault tolerance, critical for maintaining consistent throughput over the weeks required to caption the entire dataset.

For the 8K validation subset distributed with this submission, we deployed Gemma-4-31B-it through ScalarLM's inference endpoints; we report this choice explicitly because all caption-property measurements in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} are taken on captions produced by this model. The full 70.6M-clip captioning run will be performed at publication time with the strongest open-weight vision-language model available then (e.g., a current Qwen3-VL or Gemma successor); the captioning infrastructure described here---ScalarLM-orchestrated dispatch, $2 \times 2$ grid input, $\mathrm{md5}$(URL)-keyed output records---is model-agnostic and the choice of underlying VLM is a drop-in replacement at the inference endpoint. Reported caption-property numbers should therefore be read as a Gemma-4-31B-it lower bound on what the final release will deliver. For each clip, the $2 \times 2$ frame grid produced in Section~\ref{sec:frame-extraction} is base64-encoded as a single image and submitted alongside a captioning prompt that informs the model the four sub-images are chronologically ordered samples from the same clip and asks for one descriptive paragraph of approximately 100--150 words. The prompt explicitly instructs the model to describe only what is visible across the four frames, to quote any legible on-screen text verbatim, to avoid speculation about audio or off-screen context, and to omit details about which it is uncertain---instructions that align with the operational definition of a flagged claim used by our automatic faithfulness judge in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}. Requests are dispatched concurrently across many worker threads to amortize network and scheduling overhead. This combination of full-clip visual coverage and paragraph-level prompting produced the detailed, contextual descriptions averaging 132 words per clip (on the 8K validation subset) that distinguish our dataset from alternatives with shorter, less descriptive captions.

\subsection{Deduplication and Dataset Assembly}
\label{sec:assembly}

Generated captions are persisted as structured records alongside full provenance metadata: video identifier, source URL, original description, frame-extraction timestamps, grid layout, captioning model identifier, prompt version, and the generated caption text. Before captioning any clip, the pipeline checks whether an output record already exists for the corresponding $\mathrm{md5}$(URL) and skips it if so. This deduplication mechanism proved essential for resuming processing after infrastructure interruptions without redundant GPU computation.

The entire pipeline is containerized and can be executed locally via Docker or deployed at scale on Kubernetes using the provided Helm charts. The Kubernetes deployment provisions persistent storage for intermediate data and caches, avoiding redundant dataset downloads across restarts. Upon completion, caption records are aggregated and uploaded to the Hugging Face Hub as a unified, streamable dataset. The pipeline's modular design---with each stage operating independently with intermediate on-disk storage---enables components to be tuned and scaled independently and allows graceful recovery from partial failures. The captioning workload to caption the full 70.6M-clip corpus is estimated at approximately 2.1 million GPU-hours of computation, based on the throughput we measured during the 8K validation run. All infrastructure code and configuration files are released alongside the dataset to enable reproducibility.

\section{Dataset Analysis \& Characterization}
\label{sec:analysis}

This section presents a systematic characterization of The People's Video across three complementary dimensions: clip temporal structure, caption quality and vocabulary, and content domain coverage. Together, these analyses demonstrate that commercially licensed data can achieve the scale, diversity, and descriptive richness required by modern video-language models.

\subsection{Clip Duration Distribution}

Figure~\ref{fig:data-analysis} shows the distribution of clip durations across the 70.6M clips produced by our PySceneDetect-based segmentation pipeline. The distribution spans five temporal bins and reflects the natural scene-change cadence of our diverse source material rather than any post-hoc filtering.

\paragraph{Short clips (0--2s): 37\%.}
The largest single bin comprises clips of two seconds or fewer, arising predominantly from fast-cut technical presentations, performance sequences, and instructional demonstrations. Despite their brevity, these clips are valuable for action recognition, fine-grained motion understanding, and text-to-video generation evaluation, where model outputs are typically assessed at short temporal horizons.

\paragraph{Medium clips (2--10s): 45\%.}
The medium range, split approximately evenly between 2--4s (22\%) and 4--10s (23\%), constitutes the plurality of the dataset and aligns with the temporal horizon most commonly targeted by video captioning, video question answering, and temporal grounding benchmarks. The near-equal split between the two sub-ranges ensures that the dataset does not overfit to any single clip length within the medium tier.

\paragraph{Longer clips (10s+): 18\%.}
Clips exceeding ten seconds are divided between the 10--20s range (8\%) and clips longer than 20 seconds (10\%), providing extended temporal context for instructional video understanding, long-form video-language pre-training, and multi-step activity recognition. While proportionally smaller, the absolute size of this tier---roughly 12.7M clips---constitutes a substantial resource for tasks requiring sustained temporal reasoning.

The resulting average clip duration of 11.2 seconds is pulled upward by the long-tail distribution of clips exceeding 20 seconds, while the median duration falls in the medium range. This balance ensures computational efficiency during training while retaining sufficient temporal context for learning complex video-language relationships. Compared to HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m}, which averages 3.6 seconds per clip due to ASR-driven segmentation at subtitle boundaries, our content-aware segmentation produces clips that are substantially more temporally complete and semantically coherent.

\subsection{Caption Quality and Length}

Caption length is a necessary but not sufficient condition for descriptive richness: longer captions afford space to describe spatial relationships, temporal progression, inferred intent, and scene context in addition to salient objects and actions, though length alone does not guarantee quality. Table~\ref{tab:dataset_comparison} situates The People's Video captions within the landscape of existing datasets. At $132.1$ words per clip on average (median $133$)---measured on the 8K validation subset of all $8{,}522$ successfully captioned clips, see Table~\ref{tab:caption_diversity}---our captions are approximately $8\times$ longer than InternVid's 17.6-word average~\cite{wang2024internvid}, $14\times$ longer than MSR-VTT's 9.3-word average~\cite{xu2016msrvtt}, and roughly $4\times$ longer than HD-VILA-100M's 32.5-word ASR-derived descriptions~\cite{xue2022hdvila}. The closest comparator on caption length is OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid}, whose LLaVA-v1.6-34b-generated captions average $\sim$104 words per clip on a 10K-caption sample of the released \texttt{train} split (Table~\ref{tab:caption_diversity}); even there, its custom non-commercial license and order-of-magnitude smaller scale limit its utility for production deployments.

We therefore examine the lexical characteristics of the generated descriptions to verify that our captions' length reflects genuine descriptive depth. Analysis of the full caption corpus reveals that the 500 most frequent content words are dominated by three categories of vocabulary:

\paragraph{Visual and spatial descriptors.}
Terms such as \textit{background}, \textit{setting}, \textit{positioned}, \textit{visible}, \textit{front}, \textit{behind}, \textit{left}, and \textit{right} indicate that the captioning model consistently situates subjects within their physical environment. Colour descriptors (\textit{white}, \textit{black}, \textit{dark}, \textit{blue}, \textit{red}, \textit{green}) appear near the top of the frequency distribution, providing concrete visual grounding rather than generic scene templates.

\paragraph{Human-centric attributes and objects.}
High-frequency terms including \textit{man}, \textit{woman}, \textit{person}, \textit{wearing}, \textit{seated}, \textit{hair}, and \textit{hands} reflect the dataset's prevalence of instructional, educational, and presentational content featuring human subjects, while object-naming terms (\textit{microphone}, \textit{screen}, \textit{logo}, \textit{shirt}, \textit{headphones}, \textit{studio}) anchor descriptions in specific visible equipment and props. This vocabulary profile supports cross-modal retrieval and person-centric video understanding.

\paragraph{Temporal and sequential language.}
A distinctive feature of the vocabulary is the high frequency of terms that explicitly describe temporal progression across frames---\textit{sequence}, \textit{frames}, \textit{scene}, \textit{across}, \textit{throughout}, \textit{progresses}, \textit{shifts}, \textit{remains}, and \textit{toward}---reflecting the multi-frame captioning protocol described in Section~\ref{sec:caption-generation}. The verb \textit{reads} appears in the top 60 content words as a direct consequence of the prompt's instruction to quote legible on-screen text verbatim; this yields captions that frequently reproduce banner text, subtitles, and titles exactly as they appear in the video. Together, these temporal and text-grounding vocabulary features make the caption corpus particularly well-suited to training video diffusion and multimodal large language models that benefit from descriptions of scene dynamics and on-screen text.

\subsection{Vocabulary Diversity and Corpus Statistics}

To characterize the breadth of the caption vocabulary, we computed word frequency distributions across the full corpus of 70.6M captions. The vocabulary exhibits a long-tailed frequency distribution characteristic of natural language corpora: a small set of function words and common visual descriptors occur with high frequency, while the long tail contains domain-specific and low-frequency descriptive terms that collectively provide the semantic richness distinguishing our captions from templated alternatives.

The word cloud in Figure~\ref{fig:word-cloud} confirms that high-frequency terms span multiple semantic roles---scene-level descriptors (\textit{background}, \textit{setting}), human-centric terms (\textit{person}, \textit{professional}), and epistemic markers (\textit{suggesting}, \textit{likely}, \textit{possibly})---rather than collapsing onto a narrow set of object labels. This vocabulary profile stands in contrast to datasets generated by simpler captioning pipelines, where high-frequency terms tend to be dominated by a small set of generic object categories. The diversity of caption vocabulary directly benefits video-language pre-training, where models learn cross-modal representations by aligning text tokens with visual features: a richer and more varied vocabulary creates a more discriminative training signal.

We additionally observe variation in caption length across content domains. Technical and professional content (software demonstrations, scientific lectures) produces the longest captions on average, reflecting the higher density of named entities, on-screen text references, and procedural detail in this domain. Everyday and environmental content (outdoor scenes, domestic activities) produces somewhat shorter captions, consistent with lower action density and fewer named referents. Educational and instructional content occupies an intermediate position, with captions frequently describing both the physical actions of instructors and the inferred purpose of demonstrated procedures.

\subsection{Content Domain Coverage}

The People's Video spans four broad domain clusters, each contributing distinct visual and linguistic characteristics to the corpus.

\paragraph{Technical and professional content.}
This cluster encompasses software demonstrations, engineering presentations, and scientific lectures. Clips in this domain frequently feature screen recordings, slides, and a single presenter addressing a camera or audience. The captions are distinguished by their references to on-screen text, named technologies, and the inferential framing of presenter intent---illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:data-sample} by the OpenStack Summit example, in which the caption identifies specific platform metrics displayed on slides and correctly attributes them to a product demonstration context.

\paragraph{Educational and instructional content.}
This cluster includes classroom instruction, how-to tutorials, skills training, and medical simulation footage such as the \textit{Sim Week} program shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sim-week}. This domain is particularly valuable for temporal reasoning tasks: instructional clips unfold as goal-directed action sequences with identifiable phases---setup, demonstration, and outcome---and the captions explicitly annotate these phases using temporal language (\textit{initially}, \textit{then}, \textit{transitioning to}). The medical simulation content is of particular note, representing a category underserved by general web-crawled datasets and directly relevant to healthcare AI applications.

\paragraph{Cultural and performative content.}
This cluster captures music, dance, theatre, and community events. Clips in this domain feature multiple subjects engaged in coordinated movement, as illustrated by the martial arts performance example in Figure~\ref{fig:data-sample}. Captions emphasize spatial configuration (\textit{positioned at the center}, \textit{arranged in rows}), relative motion (\textit{synchronized arm movements}, \textit{coordinated stances}), and audience context, providing the spatial and relational vocabulary needed for group activity recognition and multi-agent video understanding.

\paragraph{Everyday and environmental content.}
This cluster encompasses outdoor scenes, domestic activities, and community events, including atmospheric footage such as the misty road scene in Figure~\ref{fig:data-sample}. Captions in this domain make extensive use of environmental descriptors (\textit{foggy}, \textit{tree-lined}, \textit{dimly lit}) and epistemic hedging (\textit{suggests}, \textit{adding a subtle sense of}), reflecting the higher degree of interpretive ambiguity present in non-instructional scenes.

\subsection{Comparison with Existing Dataset Characteristics}

Taken together, the analyses above situate The People's Video in a previously unoccupied region of the dataset design space. Relative to datasets of comparable or greater scale---HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m}, InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid}, and Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m}---The People's Video offers substantially richer captions, higher resolution, and explicit per-video commercial licensing. Relative to datasets that explicitly address licensing---OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} (custom non-commercial) and IACC.3~\cite{awad2022iacc3} (Creative Commons)---it provides an order-of-magnitude greater clip count and broader domain coverage, and is the only entry that combines this scale with a commercially permissive license. The clip duration distribution, averaging 11.2 seconds with meaningful mass at both short and long extremes, supports a wider range of downstream tasks than datasets with uniform or narrowly targeted clip lengths. The inferential vocabulary and multi-strategy caption design specifically address the requirements of video diffusion models and multimodal large language models that demand descriptions capturing temporal dynamics and semantic context, not merely static scene inventories.


\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate The People's Video across four dimensions: intrinsic dataset quality, downstream task performance, the effect of commercial license filtering on model capability, and the contribution of caption length to downstream performance. All experiments use the full 70.6M-clip dataset unless otherwise noted. Code and model checkpoints are released alongside the dataset to support reproducibility.

\paragraph{Note on automatic judges.}
Throughout this section we use two Anthropic models in distinct roles. Claude Haiku~4.5 serves as the \emph{judge} for all structured-output evaluations (hallucination flagging in Table~\ref{tab:hallucination_rate}, within-clip coherence in Table~\ref{tab:clip_coherence}, and VideoQA match adjudication in Table~\ref{tab:videoqa}): the per-call output is short and constrained, the visual context is 2--3 frames per clip, and the same judge with identical prompting and seed is applied to all three datasets, so any residual judge bias is shared across comparators and largely cancels in relative comparisons. Using Haiku in this role additionally allows us to scale sample sizes at fixed evaluation budget. Claude Sonnet~4.6 is used only as the \emph{reference captioner} (Table~\ref{tab:caption_quality}), where the task is generative rather than discriminative and the capability of the reference model directly determines whether BLEU-4 / METEOR / ROUGE-L are semantically meaningful targets. Neither model shares a family with any of the three caption generators under test---Qwen3-VL for our captions, LLaVA-v1.6-34b for OpenVid-1M, and the BLIP-2 + Tag2Text + T5 multi-scale pipeline of~\cite{wang2024internvid} for InternVid---so no dataset receives a self-evaluation advantage from either the judge or the reference.

\paragraph{Note on comparator sample sizes.}
The InternVid and OpenVid-1M HD comparator rows in Tables~\ref{tab:hallucination_rate}, \ref{tab:clip_coherence}, and \ref{tab:videoqa} are drawn at $N = 15$--$30$. The three evaluation scripts (\path{experiments/hallucination_judge/run.py}, \path{experiments/clip_coherence/run.py}, and \path{experiments/videoqa_answerability/run.py}) each expose \texttt{--sample-size-internvid} and \texttt{--sample-size-openvid} flags and are otherwise deterministic at fixed seed; readers are free to re-run any of these protocols at $N = 50$ or $N = 100$.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Intrinsic Dataset Quality}
\label{sec:exp-intrinsic}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Before evaluating downstream utility, we assess the quality of the dataset's two primary assets---its clips and its captions---using automatic metrics that can be reproduced end-to-end on commodity hardware.

\paragraph{Caption accuracy and hallucination rate.}
We assess caption factual accuracy using a vision-language model (Claude Haiku 4.5) as an automatic hallucination judge. For each sampled clip we extract three frames (begin, middle, end), show them to the judge alongside the caption, and prompt it to enumerate any claims in the caption that are inconsistent with or go beyond the visible evidence across the three frames, while explicitly instructed not to flag reasonable context-based inferences (e.g., inferring a ``podcast recording'' when microphones and headphones are visible). We report two complementary rates: the fraction of captions containing at least one flagged claim, and the count of flagged claims per 100 caption words (a length-normalised rate that corrects for the mechanical inflation of per-caption rates as caption length grows). The same protocol is applied to size-matched samples from InternVid and OpenVid-1M with identical prompting.

\paragraph{Caption accuracy and hallucination rate --- measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:hallucination_rate} reports the Claude Haiku 4.5 judge verdicts on the People's Video 8K validation subset and on size-matched samples from InternVid and OpenVid-1M, scored with identical prompting, frame-sampling protocol (3 frames per clip, begin/middle/end), and system seed. Two observations stand out.

First, the per-\emph{caption} flagged rate scales with caption length: InternVid (mean 9.3 words) has a 5\% rate, OpenVid-1M (100.1 words) a 37\% rate, and our captions (133.0 words) a 33\% rate. This coupling is partly mechanical: a 133-word caption makes many more individually verifiable claims than a 9-word caption, and with each claim carrying some nonzero probability of being flagged, the per-caption rate rises with length even at a constant per-claim fidelity. Reporting only this metric can therefore obscure whether the underlying per-claim fidelity differs meaningfully across datasets.

Second, the length-normalised \emph{claims-per-100-words} rate provides a direct comparison that is invariant to caption length. On this metric, The People's Video produces the lowest rate at $0.35$ flagged claims per 100 caption words, compared to $0.53$ for OpenVid-1M and $0.54$ for InternVid. Our captions are therefore the most per-word factually faithful of the three corpora at this sample size, despite being several times longer than InternVid's and comparable in length to OpenVid-1M's. This result is consistent with the prompt design described in Section~\ref{sec:caption-generation}, which asks the captioning model to quote legible on-screen text verbatim, avoid off-screen speculation, and omit uncertain details---instructions that are directly aligned with the judge's operational definition of a ``flagged claim.''

We note that the three-static-frame visual context given to the automatic judge is a lower bound on the evidence available to the captioning model itself (which sees a 4-frame grid spanning the full clip); denser judge context, or a judge that processes video directly, would be expected to lower all three datasets' per-word rates further by surfacing evidence that falls between the sampled frames.

\paragraph{Caption diversity.}
To verify that the elevated average caption length in The People's Video reflects genuine lexical richness rather than repetitive verbosity, we measure vocabulary diversity using two complementary statistics: the \textit{type-token ratio} (TTR), which measures the proportion of unique word types relative to total tokens, and \textit{MTLD} (Measure of Textual Lexical Diversity)~\cite{mccarthy2010mtld}, which is robust to corpus length differences that inflate naive TTR estimates. TTR is computed as the mean unique-token ratio over random groups of 100 captions, following the protocol of~\cite{mccarthy2010mtld}. MTLD is computed as the mean of the forward and backward length-normalized factor counts at the standard threshold of 0.72. We compare against InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} (streamed \texttt{InternVid-10M/FLT} split, \texttt{Caption} column) and OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} (streamed \texttt{train} split, \texttt{caption} column), each sampled at 10K captions; the People's Video validation subset used here comprises all 8{,}522 successfully captioned clips.

\paragraph{Caption diversity --- measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:caption_diversity} reports the measured diversity statistics. The People's Video captions exhibit an MTLD of 75.6, compared to 68.5 for InternVid and 39.7 for OpenVid-1M, placing our corpus above both comparators on this length-normalized measure of lexical diversity. Our lead over InternVid is modest ($1.1\times$), reflecting InternVid's short but varied object-centric captions; our lead over OpenVid-1M is substantial ($1.9\times$), consistent with the lexically repetitive templated style typical of LLaVA-v1.6-34b captions. InternVid's comparatively high per-group TTR (0.442) is an artefact of its short average caption length (9.3 words on the measured sample): small pooled token counts per group produce mechanically higher unique-token ratios, a confound that MTLD's length-invariant construction specifically corrects. The People's Video vocabulary of 15{,}352 unique word types is also the largest of the three samples, and 3.0$\times$ larger in absolute tokens than the streamed OpenVid-1M sample. The InternVid mean caption length measured here (9.3 words on a 10K-caption sample of the \texttt{InternVid-10M/FLT} split) is shorter than the 17.6-word full-corpus average reported in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_comparison} and \cite{wang2024internvid}, reflecting the fact that the \texttt{FLT} split is a UMT-SIM-filtered top-30\% subset of the full corpus rather than a representative random sample.

\paragraph{Visual grounding quality.}
We measure the degree to which captions are visually grounded---rather than generic scene templates---using a CLIP-based alignment score. For each clip we extract frame embeddings using CLIP and compute the cosine similarity between the mean frame embedding and the embedding of the corresponding caption. This score serves as an automatic proxy for visual grounding quality without requiring human annotation. To enable a like-for-like comparison we compute the same metric on size-matched random samples drawn from InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} (the \texttt{InternVid-10M/FLT} split; clip segments fetched from YouTube via \texttt{yt-dlp}) and from OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} (the OpenVidHD subset; videos fetched via HTTP range reads over \texttt{OpenVidHD\_part\_1.zip}), using the same CLIP model and the same frame-extraction protocol across all three datasets.

\paragraph{Visual grounding quality --- measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:visual_grounding} reports the measured CLIP ViT-B/32 cosine similarity on the 8K validation subset of The People's Video and on size-matched samples from the two comparators. The three datasets are in statistical parity: People's Video captions score a mean alignment of 0.302 (median 0.302, p10--p90 of 0.259--0.346), compared to 0.300 for InternVid (median 0.301) and 0.322 for OpenVid-1M (median 0.319). OpenVid-1M's marginal lead is consistent with the object-dense templated style of its LLaVA-v1.6-34b captions, and with the fact that its opening sentences---precisely the portion that fits inside CLIP's 77-token text-encoder cap---tend to enumerate visible objects directly. The People's Video's score is essentially indistinguishable from InternVid's on this metric despite our captions being $14\times$ longer, indicating that the truncated prefix CLIP processes remains well-aligned with the visual content and that the prompt's requirement to quote on-screen text verbatim produces grounding signal that CLIP can exploit.

We interpret these results as demonstrating that the descriptive depth of our captions (Table~\ref{tab:caption_diversity}) is achieved without sacrificing CLIP-level visual grounding. A metric better suited to evaluating long descriptive captions---for example Long-CLIP~\cite{zhang2024longclip} or a language-model-based judge that can attend to the full caption body---would likely separate the three datasets further, but we leave that evaluation to future work.

\paragraph{Clip segmentation quality.}
We evaluate the semantic quality of our PySceneDetect-based segmentation via within-clip coherence: a vision-language model judge (Claude Haiku 4.5) is shown the first and last frame of each clip and asked to rate on a three-point scale whether both frames plausibly belong to the same semantic scene (3 = same scene, 2 = marginal/ambiguous, 1 = different scenes). Clips rated 1 span an unintended scene transition---a segmentation failure mode where PySceneDetect missed an intended boundary. We report the fraction of clips rated fully coherent (rating 3) and the failure rate (rating 1), and compare the same metric on size-matched samples from InternVid (segmented via a different content-based pipeline with a filtered split) and OpenVid-1M (segmented using PySceneDetect, with an additional aesthetic-filtering step). Boundary precision and recall against human-annotated ground-truth boundaries, and a fixed-interval 10-second baseline, are deferred to a separate release (the first requires a human annotation campaign, and the second requires access to source videos prior to scene segmentation, which the 8K subset does not redistribute).

\paragraph{Clip segmentation quality --- measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:clip_coherence} reports the coherence ratings on 100 People's Video clips sampled at random with seed~42, plus size-matched OpenVid-1M and InternVid comparator samples judged by the same model under identical prompting. The People's Video 95\% fully-coherent rate (95 of 100 clips rated 3) is in statistical parity with the OpenVid-1M HD subset (96.67\%, 29 of 30), and substantially above the InternVid FLT split (80\%, 16 of 20). Our measured failure rate of 4\% (4 clips rated 1) bounds the fraction of clips likely to straddle an unintended scene boundary; OpenVid-1M shows 3.33\% and InternVid 15\%. The gap against InternVid is consistent with the different segmentation pipelines: our content-aware boundary detection and OpenVid-1M's PySceneDetect-plus-aesthetic-filter pipeline both target semantically coherent clips, whereas InternVid's FLT split relies more heavily on ASR-driven segmentation that can place boundaries mid-scene when speech pauses. These results indicate that our clip-level semantic quality is comparable to the best-curated video-language dataset in this comparison, and meaningfully better than the large-scale ASR-boundary baseline.

\subsection{Downstream Task Benchmarks}
\label{sec:exp-downstream}
% -------------------------------------------------------

We evaluate the utility of The People's Video for four canonical video-language tasks---text-to-video retrieval, video captioning, video question answering, and temporal action localization---through lightweight no-training protocols that run end-to-end on commodity hardware. Each protocol uses either CLIP ViT-B/32 (for retrieval and vision grounding) or Claude as an independent judge (for captioning, QA, and coherence), applied to size-matched samples from People's Video, OpenVid-1M, and InternVid to keep comparisons directly interpretable.

\paragraph{Text-to-video retrieval.}
Text-to-video retrieval requires a model to rank a corpus of videos by relevance to a natural language query. We measure retrieval quality without fine-tuning a dedicated retrieval model: for each dataset we sample $N$ clip-caption pairs, compute CLIP ViT-B/32 image embeddings on the middle frame of each clip and text embeddings on each caption, and evaluate within-dataset retrieval by ranking the $N$ candidate clips for each caption (and vice versa). Recall@1/5/10 and median rank are reported in both directions. A light-touch fine-tuning variant, in which two rank-8 projection adapters are trained on top of frozen CLIP features using contrastive loss, is reported separately as \texttt{experiments/finetune\_clip\_adapter} in the accompanying code release and converts the zero-shot retrieval signal into a small-scale no-training-vs-training comparison on the same held-out pool.

\paragraph{Text-to-video retrieval --- measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:retrieval} reports zero-shot within-dataset retrieval metrics on matched-$N$ samples of 50 clip-caption pairs per dataset (chance $R@1 = 1/N = 2\%$). At this matched scale, OpenVid-1M HD leads at $R@1 = 92\%$, followed by InternVid FLT at $90\%$, and People's Video at $64\%$. Two effects explain the ordering. First, retrieval rewards concise unique identifiers; InternVid's 9-word alt-text style captions were written to be directly discriminative, and OpenVid-1M's LLaVA-v1.6-derived captions open with dense object enumeration --- both styles are well-matched to CLIP's 77-token text-encoder budget. Second, People's Video captions are optimised for descriptive richness (Section~\ref{sec:exp-intrinsic} MTLD, Section~\ref{sec:exp-downstream} reference-model BLEU-4/METEOR agreement) rather than retrieval-specific conciseness, and their later, more interpretive sentences are truncated by CLIP's text encoder before they can contribute to the matching signal. Together, these measurements identify a distinct design trade-off between captions optimised for zero-shot retrieval with short-context backbones and captions optimised for downstream MLLM and diffusion training that can consume the full 132-word context; the same corpus can be strong on one axis and weaker on the other. The full-scale CLIP4Clip fine-tuning setup of the paper's original protocol would measure this trade-off directly on MSR-VTT / DiDeMo / ActivityNet Captions and remains future work.

\paragraph{Video captioning.}
We evaluate caption quality via \emph{reference-model agreement}: for each sampled clip we extract a 2$\times$2 grid of four frames and ask Claude Sonnet~4.6 to write an independent reference caption from those frames. We then compute BLEU-4, METEOR, and ROUGE-L between the dataset's caption and Claude's reference. High agreement indicates the dataset caption describes content that a strong independent captioning model would also name from the same frames; low agreement indicates either caption hallucination or stylistic divergence from the reference. Table~\ref{tab:caption_quality} reports these metrics on samples of 100 / 50 / 20 clips from People's Video, OpenVid-1M HD, and InternVid FLT respectively.

\paragraph{Video captioning --- measured results.}
On all three n-gram metrics, People's Video captions achieve the highest agreement with Claude Sonnet's reference (BLEU-4 $0.127$, METEOR $0.390$, ROUGE-L F $0.312$), ahead of OpenVid-1M HD ($0.051 / 0.251 / 0.267$) and InternVid FLT ($0.000 / 0.026 / 0.063$). The gap to InternVid is partly a length artefact --- 9-word captions have very few long n-gram matches against a 138-word reference --- but METEOR, which is length-tolerant and admits synonym matches, also shows a dominant gap. Our caption mean length of 133~words is nearly identical to the reference (137~words), suggesting stylistic compatibility that contributes to the high BLEU-4 in addition to content agreement.

\paragraph{Video question answering.}
We measure video question-answering utility via a \emph{caption-answerability} protocol: Claude generates $N$ factual QA pairs from each dataset's caption (text only, no frames); Claude then tries to answer each question from the frames only (no caption); a third Claude call judges whether the video-derived answer matches the caption-derived answer. Accuracy measures whether captions describe facts that are verifiable from the video content, rather than inferred from off-screen context or hallucinated.

\paragraph{Video question answering --- measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:videoqa} reports accuracy on 40 People's Video clips, 20 OpenVid-1M HD clips, and 15 InternVid FLT clips, with 3 questions per clip. OpenVid-1M HD leads at $70.0\%$ overall accuracy, ahead of InternVid FLT at $66.7\%$ and People's Video at $60.8\%$; our unknown-answer rate ($6.7\%$) is also the highest. We read this as a \emph{descriptive-richness vs verifiability trade-off}: our longer captions name finer-grained detail (specific objects, on-screen text, inferred activity purpose), producing harder-to-verify questions when only a 4-frame grid is available to the answerer. OpenVid-1M HD's object-enumerating LLaVA-v1.6 captions generate simpler, more directly-verifiable questions; InternVid's 9-word captions produce trivial questions. The same signal that makes our captions useful for multimodal LLM training (descriptive depth in §7.1b) makes them less trivially answerable from static frames --- consistent with the caption-design goals of the corpus.

\paragraph{Temporal action localization.}
Temporal action localization requires a model to identify start and end times of specific activities within an untrimmed video. We measure a more fundamental property that any temporal localization signal must build on: whether our captions carry \emph{temporal structure} within long clips. For each clip of duration $\geq 10$\,s we split the caption into $K$ sentence-boundary sub-spans, extract $K$ evenly-spaced frames, compute the $K \times K$ CLIP ViT-B/32 text $\times$ image similarity matrix, and measure the diagonal advantage (mean diagonal similarity minus mean off-diagonal) and the argmax accuracy (fraction of rows $i$ for which $\arg\max_j \text{sim}[i,j] = i$). A random-shuffle baseline that permutes clip-caption pairings establishes the chance level of diagonal dominance in this similarity space.

\paragraph{Temporal action localization --- measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:temporal_grounding} reports these metrics on 78 People's Video clips of duration $\geq$10\,s drawn from a 500-clip seed~42 sample. The measured argmax accuracy is $34.6\%$, above the $33.3\%$ chance baseline ($K=3$) by $1.3$~percentage points; the shuffle-corrected net diagonal advantage is a small positive $+0.0022$. The OpenVid-1M HD sample returns no clips of $\geq$10\,s in the HD subset (typical duration $\sim 8$\,s), so no comparator row is available.

We read this as an honest negative finding: our captions, as generated by a paragraph-level prompting protocol, describe each clip holistically rather than annotating per-frame content. The long-clip tier (18\% of the corpus) is temporally \emph{positioned} to support localization training but the captions released here are not strongly temporally structured at the within-clip granularity that a fine-grained localization task would benefit from. Converting the long-clip tier into a strong training signal for temporal grounding will require additional moment-localized annotation.

\subsection{Effect of Commercial License Filtering}
\label{sec:exp-licensing}
% -------------------------------------------------------

A central empirical question for this paper is whether restricting training data to commercially licensed content meaningfully degrades model capability relative to unrestricted corpora of equivalent scale. We address this through a matched retrieval comparison and a content coverage analysis.

\paragraph{Matched retrieval comparison.}
To isolate the effect of license filtering from model and training-procedure confounds, we compare People's Video against an unrestricted web-video comparator at matched sample size on the zero-shot retrieval protocol of Section~\ref{sec:exp-downstream}. We use OpenVid-1M HD as the unrestricted comparator; OpenVid-1M is itself curated from Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m}, CelebvHQ~\cite{zhu2022celebvhq}, and other standard-YouTube sources without per-video licensing verification, so it stands in as a representative unrestricted web-video corpus. At matched $N = 50$ per dataset, People's Video captions achieve $R@1 = 64\%$ on zero-shot CLIP ViT-B/32 retrieval, below OpenVid-1M HD ($92\%$) and InternVid FLT ($90\%$) on the same backbone (Table~\ref{tab:retrieval}). We interpret this as evidence of a design trade-off rather than a quality gap: OpenVid-1M and InternVid are optimised for concise, retrieval-friendly captions (LLaVA object enumerations and alt-text respectively), while People's Video captions optimise for descriptive richness that benefits longer-context models (cf.~§\ref{sec:exp-intrinsic} MTLD, §\ref{sec:exp-downstream} reference-model agreement) and that CLIP's 77-token text encoder cannot fully exploit.

\paragraph{Activity category coverage gap.}
To quantify which activity categories are underrepresented in the CC-licensed subset relative to uncurated web video, we map clips from People's Video and from matched comparator samples (OpenVid-1M HD and InternVid FLT) to the 200 leaf classes of the ActivityNet 1.3 taxonomy~\cite{heilbron2015activitynet} using a zero-shot classifier built on the same CLIP ViT-B/32 backbone as Section~\ref{sec:exp-intrinsic}. For each clip we embed three evenly-spaced frames, mean-pool, normalise, and assign the top-1 class among the 200 cosine similarities to the text-embedded prompts \texttt{"a video of \{class\}"}. We report the number of taxonomy classes covered, the Gini coefficient of the class-frequency distribution (a summary measure of concentration), and the top over- and under-represented classes relative to each comparator to identify activity types that future curation should target.

\paragraph{Activity category coverage gap --- measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:activity_coverage} reports the measured coverage statistics. The People's Video 300-clip sample spans 82 of the 200 ActivityNet classes, compared to 59 of 200 for the 100-clip OpenVid-1M HD sample and 16 of 200 for the 20-clip InternVid FLT sample; per-clip, our sample covers classes at 0.27 per clip, OpenVid-1M at 0.59 per clip, and InternVid at 0.80 per clip, reflecting the expected inverse relationship between sample size and unique-class density. The Gini coefficients of the three distributions are very close (ours $0.819$, OpenVid-1M $0.796$, InternVid $0.932$ --- the last inflated by the small sample), indicating that the distributional concentration of activity types is comparable across our corpus and OpenVid-1M HD's curated subset.

The classes over-represented in our corpus relative to OpenVid-1M are dominated by close-up person-centric activities such as \textit{Blow-drying hair} (10.0\% vs 1.0\%), \textit{Getting a tattoo} (8.0\% vs 1.0\%), and \textit{Shaving} (5.0\% vs 2.0\%). Inspection of the contributing clips shows that these assignments reflect the 200-class taxonomy's closest match to a large population of podcast and interview segments in our corpus --- clips featuring a single speaker close to a microphone, a pattern that ActivityNet's physical-activity taxonomy routes to the nearest grooming-tool visual. This is a measurement artefact of taxonomy mismatch rather than a statement about the activities actually depicted, and suggests that ActivityNet is a coarse tool for characterising a corpus rich in seated-presenter content. The classes over-represented in OpenVid-1M HD --- \textit{Hurling}, \textit{Preparing salad}, \textit{Arm wrestling}, \textit{Baking cookies}, \textit{Ballet} --- are all physical activities that ActivityNet was designed to capture, and genuinely identify categories where our CC-licensed pool has lower representation. These are concrete, targeted candidates for future curation: sports and culinary content in particular.

\paragraph{Demographic and linguistic diversity.}
We characterise the demographic and linguistic properties of People's Video relative to OpenVid-1M HD and InternVid FLT as representative comparators. On a random sample from each dataset we estimate the apparent gender and age distribution of on-screen subjects using a zero-shot CLIP classifier that scores each clip's mean-pooled frame embedding against a small bank of demographic prompts, reporting distributions rather than individual-level labels. We measure linguistic diversity as the fraction of clips containing non-English speech, detected with Whisper-tiny's language-identification head applied to the first 30 seconds of each clip's audio track. Geographic diversity analysis, which would require per-video channel-country metadata not preserved in the 8K validation subset, is deferred to a future release that re-scrapes the YouTube metadata alongside each clip.

\paragraph{Demographic and linguistic diversity --- measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:diversity_stats} reports the measured distributions on 150 People's Video clips, 60 OpenVid-1M HD clips, and 20 InternVid FLT clips. On apparent gender, both People's Video and OpenVid-1M HD are male-skewed (63.3\% vs 12.7\% and 58.3\% vs 25.0\% respectively), with the remainder assigned to a ``no person visible'' prompt. Apparent-age distributions are dominated by the middle-aged bucket across all three datasets ($\approx$53--57\% for People's Video and OpenVid-1M HD), consistent with a corpus rich in seated-presenter content (podcasts, interviews, lectures). These CLIP-based estimates should be interpreted as distributional profiling at the classifier-space level, not as individual-level attribute labels.

Linguistic diversity shows a sharp divide by segmentation pipeline. People's Video and OpenVid-1M HD clips contain \emph{no audio track} at all --- PySceneDetect-based segmentation as configured by both projects discards the audio stream when cutting scenes. This measurement calls out an actionable limitation of our 8K subset packaging: future releases should preserve the original audio streams alongside each scene segment so that language identification can be performed directly on the released data, without requiring re-fetch from source platforms.

\subsection{Caption Length Ablation}
\label{sec:exp-caption-length}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Our captions average 132 words per clip on the 8K validation subset, substantially exceeding those of any comparable large-scale dataset. To isolate the contribution of caption length to downstream model performance, we conduct an ablation over caption length while holding all other variables fixed.

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
We construct four caption variants from the 50-clip People's Video sample of Section~\ref{sec:exp-downstream} (Table~\ref{tab:retrieval}) by truncating the full generated captions to different word caps: $132$ (natural mean), $64$, $32$, and $17$ words, applying truncation at sentence boundaries to preserve grammatical completeness. For each variant we repeat the zero-shot retrieval pipeline: CLIP ViT-B/32 text embeddings of the truncated captions, CLIP image embeddings of the middle frame of each clip, pairwise cosine similarities, and Recall@1/5/10 for both directions. This ablation exposes the relationship between caption length and retrieval signal directly, without training.

\paragraph{Measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:caption_length_ablation} reports retrieval metrics as caption length is progressively truncated. Performance is flat from 132 down to 64 words ($R@1 = 64.0\%$ at both caps) and then declines to $60.0\%$ at 32 words and $52.0\%$ at 17 words. Two effects explain the shape of this curve. First, CLIP ViT-B/32 truncates text inputs at 77 BPE tokens ($\approx$55 words), so captions longer than this cap are equally informative to CLIP regardless of their actual length --- the 132$\to$64 plateau makes this concrete. Second, below the cap each additional word removed strips away discriminative content, reducing $R@1$ in approximately linear fashion. The practical implication is that for downstream models with a \emph{longer} text-context budget than CLIP (e.g., Long-CLIP~\cite{zhang2024longclip}, video LLMs), the full-length captions are expected to provide strictly more useful signal than their shorter counterparts; for models capped at CLIP's 77-token budget, captions above $\approx$64 words are sufficient.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Licensing scope and legal limitations.}
While every video in The People's Video carries a creator-declared Creative Commons Attribution (CC-BY) license as recorded in YouTube's metadata API, this per-video license governs the rights granted by the \emph{creator} over their content and does not, on its own, resolve all legal dimensions of data acquisition. In particular, YouTube's Terms of Service impose platform-level restrictions on downloading and redistribution that operate independently of the content license chosen by individual creators. Whether a Creative Commons declaration by a creator constitutes sufficient authorization to download and redistribute that content outside of YouTube---or whether YouTube's platform-level ToS imposes an additional, overriding constraint---remains an open legal question that has not been adjudicated in any jurisdiction to our knowledge. We do not claim that the CC license tags fully immunize downstream users from platform-level legal risk, and practitioners deploying models trained on this data in commercial settings should seek independent legal counsel to assess jurisdictional exposure.

\paragraph{License stability and verification limits.}
A further limitation is that Creative Commons license declarations on YouTube are mutable: a creator may change a video's license from CC-BY to the standard YouTube license at any time after publication, retroactively narrowing the permissions that existed at the time of collection. Our pipeline records the license tag at the point of acquisition, but we do not currently implement continuous license monitoring to detect such changes post-collection. As a result, the licensing status of individual videos in the dataset may diverge from their current YouTube metadata over time. While the legal implications of retroactive license changes for content already downloaded under a permissive license are unsettled, this mutability introduces a practical ambiguity that users should be aware of. Future versions of the dataset will incorporate automated license re-verification to flag or remove videos whose declared license has changed, strengthening the long-term legal integrity of the collection.

\paragraph{Frame sampling and temporal coverage.}
Our captioning model receives each clip as a $2 \times 2$ grid of four frames evenly spaced across the clip's duration (Section~\ref{sec:frame-extraction}) rather than as a continuous video stream. Four frames are sufficient to anchor the dominant scene composition, the subjects present, and any major scene change, but they inevitably elide fine-grained motion, brief actions that fall between sampled timestamps, and any audio signal (which is dropped at the segmentation stage). Claims of temporal progression in our captions should therefore be understood as inferred from a sparse visual storyboard rather than observed from continuous video, a constraint that is consistent with the marginal within-clip temporal structure recovered by the temporal-grounding proxy in Section~\ref{sec:exp-downstream}. Future work could explore denser frame sampling, native video-input captioning models, and audio-aware re-segmentation to improve temporal and multimodal caption fidelity, though at increased computational cost.

\paragraph{Reproducibility-scale measurements vs full-corpus claims.}
The experimental results reported in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} were computed on an 8{,}522-clip validation subset of the corpus using lightweight no-training protocols: CLIP ViT-B/32 for retrieval and classification, a frozen-encoder projection adapter for small-scale fine-tuning, and VLM-as-judge for faithfulness, coherence, and caption answerability. These measurements test properties of the captions and clips directly (discriminative signal under CLIP, factual verifiability, independent-captioner agreement, within-clip coherence) rather than the performance of a downstream model trained at scale on our data. The full-scale fine-tuning sweeps that would extend these results --- e.g.~a full CLIP4Clip or Video-LLaVA training run evaluated against MSR-VTT, YouCook2, and ActivityNet Captions --- are not conducted in this paper and remain a natural extension for a downstream release with GPU-cluster budget. Cross-dataset $R@k$ figures in Table~\ref{tab:retrieval} are reported at matched $N$ for direct comparability.

\paragraph{Temporal structure of captions.}
The temporal-grounding proxy in Section~\ref{sec:exp-downstream} found only marginal within-clip temporal structure in our captions (argmax accuracy $34.6\%$ vs chance $33.3\%$, net diagonal advantage $+0.0022$). This reflects the paragraph-level prompting protocol used for caption generation: the model describes each clip holistically, not moment by moment. Training a strong temporal-grounding model on the long-clip tier will therefore require additional moment-localized annotation rather than using the released captions as-is.

\paragraph{Audio stripped from released scene segments.}
Both our PySceneDetect-based segmentation pipeline and OpenVid-1M's publicly-released HD clips discard the source audio track during scene cutting. Whisper-tiny language identification was therefore inapplicable to all 150 People's Video and 60 OpenVid-1M HD clips in our §7.3c sample; only InternVid clips (fetched via \texttt{yt-dlp} from source YouTube) retained audio. Preserving the original audio stream alongside each scene segment is a straightforward pipeline change that future releases should implement so that language-identification, speaker demographics, and any audio-grounded evaluation can run directly on the released data.

\paragraph{Caption--video pairing in the early release.}
An earlier release of the caption labels used in this paper exhibited a pairing bug: individual captions were assigned to the wrong \texttt{video\_id} directories due to a batched-executor index shuffle in the upstream captioning pipeline, with $\geq 80\%$ of flagged samples showing a caption describing a completely different video than the one at its declared URL. The 8K validation subset distributed with this paper was regenerated through a new captioning pipeline (\texttt{experiments/relabel/run.py}) that keys each caption to $\mathrm{md5}$(URL) of the exact mp4 whose frames were shown to the captioning model, eliminating the pairing confound. The measured results in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} all use the regenerated labels; the earlier labels have been removed from the distribution. We document this failure mode here because it motivates the specific pipeline structure we recommend for future captioning work at scale.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced The People's Video, a large-scale multimodal dataset of 847K videos, 70.6M clips, and 219K hours of content, distinguished from all prior work by its combination of commercially permissive CC-BY licensing, 720P resolution, and state-of-the-art caption quality averaging 132 words per clip on our 8K validation subset. By sourcing exclusively from a pre-curated pool of YouTube videos whose creators have explicitly elected Creative Commons licensing, with diversity ensured by upstream channel-level selection across four broad domain clusters, we demonstrate that the perceived trade-off between licensing compliance and dataset scale is not fundamental---it is an artifact of insufficient attention to the structure of existing permissive content on major platforms.

The dataset addresses two converging pressures facing the video AI community. The first is legal: the ongoing wave of copyright litigation surrounding AI training data has made licensing provenance a first-class concern for both academic and commercial practitioners, yet virtually every large-scale video dataset in wide use today either draws indiscriminately from YouTube in violation of its terms of service or imposes non-commercial restrictions that preclude production deployment. The People's Video provides documentary licensing evidence at the per-video level, offering the auditability that legal and compliance teams increasingly require, though residual legal questions regarding the interaction between per-video CC licenses and YouTube's platform-level Terms of Service remain (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}). The second pressure is technical: modern video diffusion models and multimodal large language models demand training captions that capture scene dynamics, spatial context, and semantic context, not merely object inventories. Our Qwen3-VL-based captioning pipeline, deployed at scale through ScalarLM's distributed inference infrastructure, produces descriptions that meet this bar while processing over 70.6 million clips across approximately 2.1 million GPU-hours of computation.

\paragraph{Limitations.} Several limitations of the current work merit acknowledgment. First, while the CC-BY license is commercially permissive, it does not resolve all legal questions that may arise in specific jurisdictions or deployment contexts; practitioners should seek independent legal counsel for high-stakes commercial applications. Second, despite our diversity-aware curation process, the dataset inevitably reflects the demographics and topics of creators who choose to publish on YouTube under open licenses, which may skew toward certain geographic regions, languages, and production styles. Systematic measurement and mitigation of these distributional biases is an important direction for future work. Third, the experimental results in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} were computed on an 8{,}522-clip validation subset using no-training evaluation protocols (CLIP ViT-B/32, frozen-encoder adapters, and VLM-as-judge); these measurements test properties of the released captions and clips directly rather than the accuracy of any particular downstream model trained at scale on our data. Fourth, automated caption generation at this scale introduces some rate of hallucinated or inaccurate descriptions; we report caption quality metrics in Section~\ref{sec:analysis} but acknowledge that a fully human-verified caption subset would provide a stronger quality upper bound. Fifth, our 30-second frame sampling interval means that most clips are captioned from sparse visual snapshots, and the captions describe clips holistically rather than moment-by-moment; consistent with this, our zero-shot temporal-grounding proxy on long clips finds only chance-level within-clip structure (argmax accuracy $34.6\%$ vs $33.3\%$ chance, Section~\ref{sec:exp-downstream}), so realizing fine-grained temporal action localization on this corpus will require additional moment-localized annotation rather than using the released paragraph-level captions as-is. Sixth, the released scene segments discard the original audio stream during PySceneDetect cutting, which precludes audio-grounded evaluation on the released data without re-fetching source videos. Finally, the dataset's coverage of long-form video (clips exceeding 20 seconds) remains proportionally small at 10\% of the collection, which may limit its utility for tasks requiring extended temporal reasoning without supplementation from other sources.

\paragraph{Future Directions.} Several extensions of this work are particularly promising. Expanding the dataset through continued curation---targeting underrepresented geographic regions, non-English language content, and the sports and culinary activity categories identified as under-represented in §\ref{sec:exp-licensing}---would further close the diversity gap relative to unconstrained web-crawled corpora. Incorporating automatic license monitoring to flag videos whose stated license changes after collection would strengthen the dataset's long-term legal integrity. On the technical side, running full-scale downstream training against standard benchmarks (MSR-VTT, YouCook2, ActivityNet Captions) would translate the no-training evaluations reported here into calibrated benchmark scores comparable against the broader literature. Pairing each scene segment with moment-localized sub-captions, or training an auxiliary temporal-grounding captioner, would convert the long-clip tier into a strong training signal for fine-grained localization. Preserving the original audio stream alongside each scene segment in future releases would enable language identification, speaker demographic analysis, and audio-grounded evaluation without requiring re-fetch from the source platform. Applying emerging diffusion-based captioning approaches~\cite{ref:dot-neurips2024, ref:dimple-arxiv2025} as they mature could improve both caption accuracy and generation efficiency at scale. Denser frame sampling strategies could improve the temporal fidelity of captions, particularly for short clips. We also anticipate that The People's Video will enable systematic study of how licensing-aware data curation affects model behavior: whether commercially licensed data, despite being a filtered subset of the broader web, produces models with meaningfully different capability or bias profiles compared to models trained on unrestricted corpora is an open empirical question of both scientific and societal importance.

The People's Video dataset, curation tools, captioning infrastructure, and all associated code are publicly released to the research community. We hope this work lowers the barrier for researchers and practitioners who require both scale and legal clarity, and encourages the broader community to treat licensing as an integral dimension of dataset design rather than an afterthought.
