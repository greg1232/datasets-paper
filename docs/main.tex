\begin{abstract}
  We introduce The People's Video, a large-scale multimodal dataset comprising 847K videos with 70.6M clips and 219K hours of content under commercially permissive CC-BY licensing. Unlike existing video-language datasets that rely on restricted YouTube content or proprietary sources, our dataset provides the research community with truly open, commercially usable video data. Our dataset addresses a critical gap: datasets like InternVid and HowTo100M offer scale but lack commercial licensing clarity, while smaller datasets like MSR-VTT provide manual annotations but cannot support modern large-scale video-language models. We employ Qwen3VL for automated caption generation, producing detailed descriptions averaging 117 words per clip across 720P resolution videos with an average clip length of 11.2 seconds. The People's Video spans diverse domains including technical presentations, instructional content, educational material, and everyday activities. Clip durations are distributed across short (0-2s, 37\%), medium (2-10s, 45\%), and longer segments (10s+, 18\%), reflecting the natural scene-change cadence of diverse source material and supporting downstream tasks including text-to-video generation, video captioning, video question answering, temporal action localization, video-language pre-training, and long-form video understanding. The varied clip durations and detailed captions make the dataset particularly suitable for training video diffusion models, multimodal large language models, and video-language contrastive learning frameworks. We demonstrate that commercially licensed data can achieve the scale and quality necessary for state-of-the-art video understanding models, providing researchers and practitioners with a substantially stronger licensing foundation for commercial deployment than existing alternatives. The dataset and associated tools are publicly available to accelerate research in video-language understanding.


\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The rapid advancement of video AI has created an unprecedented demand for large-scale, high-quality video datasets. State-of-the-art video understanding models---including open-weight systems like Qwen3-VL~\cite{qwen3vxl} and proprietary systems like Gemini~\cite{gemini2023}---require massive multimodal training data spanning diverse domains to capture the rich complexity of real-world visual content. Simultaneously, video generation has emerged as one of the most rapidly evolving frontiers in generative AI: models such as OpenAI's Sora~2~\cite{sora2} and Google's Veo~3~\cite{veo3} produce photorealistic, temporally coherent video from text descriptions, demanding large-scale video-text datasets with detailed captions. Across both understanding and generation, the need for high-quality video data has never been greater.

Yet the AI research community faces an increasingly acute data challenge. Recent analyses suggest that the stock of high-quality public text data may be exhausted by 2027 under current scaling regimes~\cite{2027AJ}, and video data---requiring orders of magnitude more storage and processing---presents even greater scarcity challenges. As one analysis noted, ``the well of untapped data that fuelled the last wave of AI breakthroughs is running dry''~\cite{2027AJ}. High-quality training data has become finite, with much of the public internet already mined for existing datasets. The People's Video directly addresses this scarcity by systematically curating permissively licensed videos from YouTube---a vast and diverse source whose CC-0 and CC-BY licensed content has been largely overlooked by existing datasets.

Compounding this scarcity, the legal landscape surrounding AI training data has grown increasingly contentious. The \textit{New York Times}' landmark lawsuit against OpenAI---alleging that copyrighted articles were used without authorization to train GPT models---proceeded to trial in 2025, exemplifying a broader wave of litigation from publishers, authors, and content creators worldwide~\cite{nyt_lawsuit}. Similar actions target Anthropic, Google, and other major AI developers. These legal challenges have created significant uncertainty for practitioners: while YouTube hosts a vast corpus of video content, the platform's standard terms of service explicitly prohibit downloading and using videos for commercial AI training purposes. Crucially, however, a meaningful subset of YouTube creators publish their work under Creative Commons CC-0 (public domain dedication) or CC-BY licenses, which grant explicit permission for commercial use and redistribution with attribution. Unlike datasets such as InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} and HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m} that draw indiscriminately from YouTube without regard to per-video licensing, The People's Video was constructed by manually curating only those YouTube videos explicitly released under CC-0 or CC-BY licenses, while simultaneously prioritizing diversity of content, domain, and activity type. Together, data scarcity and licensing restrictions create a clear imperative: the field needs a dataset that is both large-scale and commercially licensed.

To address both challenges, we developed The People's Video dataset in collaboration with MLCommons, the world's leading AI benchmarking consortium. MLCommons unites over 125 member organizations---including Google, Intel, NVIDIA, Microsoft, and leading academic institutions---around a shared mission of making AI better for everyone~\cite{mlcommons2024}. These organizations require commercially licensed datasets usable in open-source software, research, and standards development without legal ambiguity, a requirement that existing large-scale video datasets fundamentally fail to meet. Beyond licensing, we contribute a scalable captioning infrastructure using Qwen3-VL to generate detailed, contextual descriptions averaging 117 words per clip---captions specifically designed to support modern video diffusion models and multimodal large language models. Our primary contributions are as follows:

\paragraph{Key Contributions.}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{First commercially licensed video dataset at modern pre-training scale.} The People's Video is the first video-language dataset combining commercial CC-BY licensing with the high resolution (720P) and scale (847K videos, 70.6M clips, 219K hours) required by modern pre-training regimes---extending prior CC-licensed collections such as IACC.3~\cite{awad2022iacc3} by over two orders of magnitude. Previous datasets achieve comparable scale only by sourcing from YouTube without license filtering, which prohibits commercial use of downloaded content under YouTube's standard terms of service. By exclusively curating YouTube videos published under CC-0 or CC-BY licenses through a diversity-aware selection process, we provide the strongest available licensing foundation for both academic research and commercial deployment, though practitioners should consult legal counsel regarding platform-level terms of service (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

    \item \textbf{Comprehensive characterization of curated YouTube CC-licensed video sources.} We present detailed analysis of the dataset including clip duration distributions (37\% short clips 0--2s, 45\% medium 2--10s, 18\% long 10s+), caption statistics (117-word average length), and vocabulary analysis through word frequency distributions. We characterize the diverse content domains spanning technical presentations, educational material, instructional content, and everyday activities, demonstrating the dataset's suitability for multiple downstream tasks.

    \item \textbf{Scalable captioning infrastructure using state-of-the-art vision-language models.} We describe our distributed pipeline integrating license-aware YouTube curation, PySceneDetect-based video segmentation, and ScalarLM-orchestrated inference with Qwen3-VL (235B parameters) for caption generation. The infrastructure processed 2.1 million GPU-hours of computation to generate detailed, contextual captions for over 70.6 million clips---substantially longer and more descriptive than existing datasets.

    \item \textbf{Caption design for emerging video generation architectures.} Our captions averaging 117 words per clip are specifically designed to support training of video diffusion models and multimodal large language models that benefit from detailed scene descriptions, spatial context, and inferential language capturing both observable elements and semantic interpretations.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Licensing in Video Datasets}
The licensing of video datasets is a critical yet often overlooked dimension of dataset design with far-reaching consequences for the research community. Many widely adopted datasets, including MSR-VTT~\cite{xu2016msrvtt}, HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m}, and WebVid-10M~\cite{bain2021frozen}, are distributed under terms that either prohibit commercial use or restrict redistribution, effectively confining their utility to academic settings only. Such constraints create significant barriers for practitioners building production systems and impede reproducibility when derived works cannot be freely redistributed. Creative Commons (CC) licenses---particularly CC-0 and CC-BY---have emerged as a practical standard for open data sharing, explicitly granting permission to use, adapt, and redistribute content with proper attribution. A notable early effort in this space is the IACC.3 dataset~\cite{awad2022iacc3}, comprising approximately 4,600 Internet Archive videos (600 hours) under Creative Commons licenses, curated by NIST and widely used in the TRECVID evaluation benchmark for video retrieval research. While IACC.3 pioneered the concept of a CC-licensed video collection, its scale---two orders of magnitude smaller than modern pre-training corpora---limits its applicability to contemporary video-language model training. The People's Video builds upon this foundational insight that permissively licensed video can be systematically curated from public platforms, but extends it to the scale demanded by modern architectures: 70.6M clips and 219K hours, representing roughly 350$\times$ more content than IACC.3.

More recently, OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} has emerged as a notable high-quality video generation dataset. However, OpenVid-1M's design priorities are fundamentally orthogonal to ours: it emphasizes \emph{visual quality filtering}---aesthetics, temporal consistency, motion smoothness, and clarity---rather than \emph{licensing provenance}. OpenVid-1M is curated from Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m}, CelebvHQ~\cite{zhu2022celebvhq}, ChronoMagic~\cite{yuan2024chronomagic}, and Open-Sora-Plan~\cite{lin2024opensoraplan}---sources drawn from standard YouTube content without any per-video license verification. The paper makes no affirmative claim of commercial usability, and the dataset's custom license restricts use to non-commercial purposes. Beyond licensing, the scale difference is substantial: OpenVid-1M contains approximately 1M clips totaling 2K hours, while The People's Video provides 70.6M clips and 219K hours---roughly 70$\times$ more clips and 100$\times$ more total content. OpenVid-1M does achieve longer average captions (467 words versus our 117 words), reflecting its use of LLaVA-v1.6-34b for captioning a much smaller corpus, but this advantage comes at the cost of both scale and commercial deployability. The People's Video thus occupies a fundamentally different position in the dataset landscape: where OpenVid-1M optimizes for per-clip visual quality at moderate scale without licensing guarantees, we optimize for licensing compliance, domain diversity, and the large-scale clip counts required for pre-training, while maintaining competitive caption quality and 720P resolution.

\paragraph{Diversity in Action and Category Coverage}
A key challenge in building video datasets for general-purpose learning is achieving broad coverage across action types and semantic categories. Narrowly scoped datasets tend to produce models that overfit to a limited set of activity types, limiting their generalizability to novel scenarios. ActivityNet~\cite{heilbron2015activitynet} offers a foundational taxonomy of hundreds of human activity classes, but the scale and open-domain breadth needed for modern learning systems requires going further. InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} explicitly addresses this by sampling video content across 16 broad topic domains using action-oriented queries spanning thousands of activity descriptions, promoting coverage across both common and rare activity types. Similarly, Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m} and YT-Temporal-180M~\cite{zellers2022merlot} highlight that without deliberate category-aware balancing, large-scale web-crawled datasets tend to be dominated by a narrow set of high-frequency activities, leaving long-tail categories severely underrepresented. Our dataset adopts explicit action- and category-aware sampling strategies to promote more uniform coverage across semantic classes, producing a distribution better suited to training robust and generalizable models across diverse scenarios.

\paragraph{Video Datasets for Generative Model Training}
The rapid advancement of text-to-video (T2V) generation models has placed fundamentally new requirements on training datasets. Landmark systems such as Sora~\cite{brooks2024sora}, Open-Sora~\cite{zheng2024opensora}, and Open-Sora Plan~\cite{lin2024opensoraplan} have demonstrated that model quality is tightly coupled not only to data scale but also to video quality, resolution, and the expressiveness of accompanying text descriptions. Earlier large-scale datasets like WebVid-10M~\cite{bain2021frozen}, while widely adopted, contain predominantly low-resolution clips with watermarks and short, imprecise captions---properties that limit their suitability as a sole training source for modern generative models. Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m} offers significantly greater scale but has been noted to include large fractions of static or low-clarity clips alongside similarly brief captions. OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} directly responds to these quality shortcomings by curating over one million high-quality clips through a rigorous multi-stage filtering pipeline---assessing aesthetics, temporal consistency, motion smoothness, and clarity---and generating expressive captions via LLaVA-v1.6-34b~\cite{liu2024llavanext}. OpenVid-1M further includes a 1080p subset (OpenVidHD-0.4M) specifically targeting high-definition generation, and demonstrates through controlled experiments that its quality-filtered data produces superior T2V generation results compared to training on unfiltered Panda-70M or watermarked WebVid-10M content. However, OpenVid-1M's quality-first approach does not address the licensing dimension: its source material is drawn from standard YouTube content without per-video license verification, precluding commercial deployment of models trained on it. Our dataset is designed with both generative training demands and licensing compliance in mind, prioritizing visual fidelity, temporal richness, caption quality, and explicit per-video licensing documentation to provide a training resource that practitioners can deploy with substantially reduced legal risk.

\section{The People's Video: A Commercially Licensed Multimodal Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

The People's Video is a large-scale multimodal dataset comprising 847K videos, 70.6M clips, and 219K hours of content, all released under commercially permissive Creative Commons licensing. It is, to our knowledge, the first video-language dataset to simultaneously achieve this scale, maintain 720P resolution, and provide explicit per-video commercial licensing documentation. In this section we describe the dataset's licensing foundation, content domains, structural properties, and caption characteristics.

\subsection{Licensing and Commercial Usability}

The central design principle of The People's Video is that every video in the collection must carry an explicit, commercially permissive Creative Commons license. Concretely, we restrict inclusion to videos published by their creators under either the CC0 1.0 Universal (public domain dedication) or CC-BY 4.0 International license. CC0 content carries no restrictions whatsoever, while CC-BY content requires only attribution to the original creator---a condition fully compatible with open-source software, published research, and commercial product development. Both licenses stand in sharp contrast to the standard YouTube Terms of Service, which prohibit downloading and reusing content for AI training, as well as to licenses such as CC-BY-NC that restrict commercial exploitation.

Critically, YouTube itself supports per-video license declaration: creators may publish their work under a standard YouTube license or elect to release it under Creative Commons. Our curation process targets exclusively the latter group. Each video in the dataset was verified at collection time to carry a machine-readable CC0 or CC-BY license tag in YouTube's metadata API response, and this metadata is preserved alongside each video record to support downstream auditability. This approach provides practitioners with the documentary evidence needed to demonstrate licensing compliance, a requirement increasingly demanded by legal and compliance teams deploying AI systems commercially. We note, however, that per-video CC licenses interact with YouTube's platform-level Terms of Service in ways that have not been fully adjudicated; see Section~\ref{sec:limitations} for a detailed discussion of residual legal considerations.

\subsection{Manual Curation and Diversity Strategy}

The People's Video incorporates human review at the channel and query-result level: automated keyword search over YouTube's CC-licensed corpus was used to surface candidate videos across a broad taxonomy of topics, and human curators reviewed batches of candidate search results to filter low-quality uploads---such as static slideshows, corrupted files, and near-duplicate re-uploads---and to ensure representation across underserved content categories. This hybrid approach mirrors the strategy employed by InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} for category-aware sampling, but extends it to the additional constraint of per-video license verification, which cannot be reliably inferred from automated signals alone. Given the dataset's scale (847K videos), individual video-level review was not exhaustive; curation operated primarily at the level of keyword queries and channel selection.

Our curation taxonomy spans four primary domain clusters: \textit{technical and professional content} (software demonstrations, engineering presentations, scientific lectures), \textit{educational and instructional content} (classroom instruction, how-to tutorials, skills training including medical simulation), \textit{cultural and performative content} (music, dance, theatre, cultural events), and \textit{everyday and environmental content} (outdoor scenes, domestic activities, community events). Within each cluster, curators followed explicit diversity checklists covering speaker demographics, geographic setting, production quality range, and activity type, ensuring that the dataset does not collapse onto the high-frequency niches that typically dominate uncurated web video corpora~\cite{chen2024panda70m, zellers2022merlot}.

\subsection{Scale and Structural Properties}

Table~\ref{tab:dataset_comparison} situates The People's Video within the landscape of existing video-language datasets. The collection spans 847K source videos with an aggregate duration of 219K hours---comparable in scale to HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m} and Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m} while substantially exceeding smaller benchmarks such as MSR-VTT~\cite{xu2016msrvtt} and YouCook2~\cite{zhou2018youcook2}. After PySceneDetect-based segmentation (described in Section~\ref{sec:infrastructure}), the dataset yields 70.6M clips with an average duration of 11.2 seconds.

Clip durations are distributed across three temporal scales, reflecting the natural scene-change structure of the diverse source material. This distribution supports a wide range of downstream tasks, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:data-analysis}. Short clips (0--2 seconds) account for 37\% of the collection and are well suited to action recognition, text-to-video generation evaluation, and fine-grained motion understanding. Medium clips (2--10 seconds) form the largest segment at 45\%---split approximately evenly between 2--4 second and 4--10 second ranges---and align with the temporal horizon most commonly targeted by video captioning and temporal grounding benchmarks. Longer segments exceeding 10 seconds constitute the remaining 18\% (8\% in the 10--20 second range and 10\% above 20 seconds), providing the extended temporal context required for instructional video understanding and long-form video-language pre-training. All source videos are retained at 720P resolution, preserving sufficient visual fidelity for training generative models without the watermarks or compression artifacts that affect lower-resolution datasets such as WebVid-10M~\cite{bain2021frozen} and HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m}.

\subsection{Caption Characteristics}

Captions were generated for each clip using Qwen3-VL~\cite{qwen3vxl}, a 235B-parameter vision-language model deployed at scale through our ScalarLM infrastructure. The resulting captions average 117 words per clip---roughly 7$\times$ longer than InternVid's 17.6-word average and 13$\times$ longer than MSR-VTT's 9.3-word average---reflecting a deliberate choice to produce descriptions that capture not only salient objects and actions but also spatial relationships, scene context, and inferential interpretations of intent and activity. For clips represented by multiple frames, captions additionally capture temporal progression. Representative captions and the vocabulary distribution across the corpus are visualized in Figures~\ref{fig:data-sample} and~\ref{fig:word-cloud}, respectively.

The caption generation prompt was designed around five complementary reasoning strategies---temporal anchor chain-of-thought, multi-granularity hierarchical reasoning, contrastive what-if probing, visual evidence citation, and iterative decomposition with self-verification---described in detail in Section~\ref{sec:reasoning}. These strategies encourage the model to ground each descriptive claim in observable visual evidence rather than resorting to generic scene templates, and to flag and correct internally inconsistent interpretations before producing a final description. The result is a caption vocabulary dominated by precise, contextual descriptors (``suggesting'', ``indicating'', ``coordinated'', ``positioned'') alongside concrete visual references, as shown in the word cloud in Figure~\ref{fig:word-cloud}.

This caption design directly targets the requirements of modern video generation architectures. Video diffusion models such as Sora~\cite{brooks2024sora} and Veo~3~\cite{veo3} benefit from training captions that describe scene dynamics, camera motion, and temporal transitions rather than static scene summaries; our average caption length and inferential vocabulary are calibrated to this requirement. Multimodal large language models similarly benefit from captions that provide semantic depth beyond simple object lists, enabling richer cross-modal alignment during pre-training.

\subsection{Comparison with Existing Datasets}

Table~\ref{tab:dataset_comparison} provides a comprehensive comparison of The People's Video against the most widely used video-language datasets. The People's Video is the only entry in this comparison that satisfies all three of the following criteria simultaneously: (1) a commercially permissive license (CC-BY or CC0), (2) scale sufficient for modern pre-training (70.6M clips, 219K hours), and (3) high-resolution content (720P). InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} and Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m} achieve comparable scale but source from YouTube under mixed licensing terms that prohibit commercial use. OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} provides long captions (averaging 467 words) but is an order of magnitude smaller and restricted to non-commercial use by its custom license, precluding commercial deployment. DiDeMo~\cite{hendricks2017didemo} includes some Creative Commons content but does not permit commercial redistribution under its NC clauses. The People's Video thus fills a clear and previously unoccupied position in the dataset landscape: a large-scale, high-resolution video-language resource with per-video commercial licensing documentation and state-of-the-art caption quality.

\section{Data Collection Infrastructure}
\label{sec:infrastructure}
The construction of The People's Video dataset required a multi-stage infrastructure pipeline integrating web-scale data acquisition, video segmentation, frame extraction, and large-scale caption generation. Our infrastructure leveraged three key open-source frameworks: a custom scraper built on the YouTube's Advanced Search and Scraping APIs~\cite{internet_archive_api} for data acquisition, PySceneDetect~\cite{castellano2014pyscenedetect} for automated video segmentation, and ScalarLM for distributed inference at scale. ScalarLM integrates the high-throughput inference capabilities of vLLM~\cite{kwon2023efficient} with the distributed training architecture of Megatron-LM~\cite{shoeybi2019megatron, narayanan2021efficient}. This modular architecture enabled us to process 847K videos totaling 219K hours of content while maintaining commercial licensing compliance and generating high-quality captions for over 70.6 million video clips.

\subsection{Data Acquisition}
\label{sec:data-acquisition}

The data acquisition phase employed a custom scraper interfacing with the YouTube Data API v3 to systematically harvest video content with commercially permissive Creative Commons licenses. YouTube exposes per-video license metadata through its \texttt{videos.list} endpoint, where creators who elect to publish under Creative Commons are assigned a machine-readable \texttt{creativeCommon} license tag distinguishable from the default \texttt{youtube} license. Our scraper specifically targeted videos carrying this tag and, within that pool, further filtered to retain only those explicitly published under CC-BY 4.0 International or CC0 1.0 Universal licenses, as declared in the video's metadata API response. This approach differs fundamentally from datasets like InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} and HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m} that download YouTube content indiscriminately, in violation of YouTube's standard Terms of Service which prohibit downloading and reusing videos for AI training without explicit per-video permission. By restricting acquisition to videos whose creators have affirmatively elected a Creative Commons license, we operate within the legal framework established by those licenses, though the interaction with YouTube's platform-level Terms of Service remains an open question (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

Candidate videos were surfaced through keyword searches spanning our curation taxonomy, using the YouTube Data API's \texttt{search.list} endpoint with the \texttt{videoLicense=creativeCommon} filter applied at query time to restrict results to the CC-licensed pool. The license tag and full metadata record---including upload date, channel identifier, title, description, and view count---were preserved alongside each downloaded video to support downstream auditability and license verification. Human curators reviewed batches of candidate search results prior to download to filter low-quality uploads such as static slideshows, corrupted files, and near-duplicate re-uploads, consistent with the diversity-aware selection process described in Section~\ref{sec:dataset}. The scraper implemented parallel download streams with rate limiting to comply with YouTube's API quota constraints while maximizing throughput. Acquired videos were organized into sharded batches and uploaded to a Hugging Face data repository, enabling streaming access during downstream processing without requiring the full dataset to reside on any single machine.

\subsection{Video Segmentation}
\label{sec:segmentation}

Video segmentation represents a critical preprocessing step that determines the temporal granularity of the dataset. We employed PySceneDetect~\cite{castellano2014pyscenedetect}, an open-source Python library that implements content-aware scene detection algorithms to automatically segment raw videos into semantically meaningful clips. We primarily utilized the \texttt{ContentDetector}, which computes frame-to-frame differences in color and intensity histograms and triggers a scene boundary when the difference exceeds a configurable threshold. This content-based approach proved particularly effective for handling the diverse content types in our dataset, from static technical presentations to dynamic performance art. Detected scene boundaries were passed to FFmpeg for efficient video splitting without re-encoding, preserving the original 720P quality. The resulting clip duration distribution---37\% short clips (0--2s), 45\% medium clips (2--10s), and 18\% longer segments (10s+)---emerged naturally from the content detection process applied to our diverse source material, rather than being imposed by post-processing filters. This distribution supports both fine-grained action recognition tasks and long-form video understanding applications.

\subsection{Frame Extraction and Temporal Sampling}
\label{sec:frame-extraction}

To prepare video clips for vision-language model captioning, we implemented a frame extraction pipeline using FFmpeg's temporal filtering capabilities. Frames are sampled from source videos at fixed 30-second intervals and saved as lossless PNG thumbnails in per-video output directories keyed by the MD5 hash of the source URL, providing deterministic, collision-resistant identifiers that enable deduplication and efficient lookup across the dataset. Because this sampling rate is coarser than the average clip duration (11.2 seconds), most clips are represented by one or two frames rather than dense temporal samples. While this limits the captioning model's ability to describe fine-grained temporal dynamics within short clips, the multi-frame batching of adjacent samples from the source video provides broader temporal context for the overall scene. Extracted frames are then grouped into sequential pairs, where each pair represents a contiguous temporal segment of the source video. Segments with fewer frames than the minimum batch size are discarded to maintain consistent input dimensions.

\subsection{Caption Generation}
\label{sec:caption-generation}

Caption generation at our scale---producing detailed descriptions for 70.6 million clips---demanded a distributed inference infrastructure capable of handling both the computational load and the orchestration complexity of large-scale vision-language model deployment. We employed ScalarLM, an open-source platform that unifies the high-performance inference capabilities of vLLM~\cite{kwon2023efficient} with the distributed training architecture of Megatron-LM~\cite{shoeybi2019megatron, narayanan2021efficient}. ScalarLM implements PagedAttention~\cite{kwon2023efficient} for efficient KV cache management, reducing memory waste during inference, while its integration with Megatron-LM's tensor and pipeline parallelism strategies allows scaling caption generation across hundreds of GPUs. The system leverages Kubernetes for flexible job scheduling and fault tolerance, critical for maintaining consistent throughput over the weeks required to caption the entire dataset.

We deployed Qwen3-VL (235B parameters) through ScalarLM's inference endpoints. For each frame batch, constituent images are base64-encoded and submitted alongside a captioning prompt instructing the model to describe the depicted video content in paragraph form, with a maximum generation length of 512 tokens. Prompts are dispatched in batches---grouping multiple videos per API call---to amortize network and scheduling overhead. This combination of multi-frame visual context and paragraph-level prompting produced the detailed, contextual descriptions averaging 117 words per clip that distinguish our dataset from alternatives with shorter, less descriptive captions.

\subsection{Deduplication and Dataset Assembly}
\label{sec:assembly}

Generated captions are persisted as structured records alongside full provenance metadata: video identifier, source URL, original description, temporal sampling parameters, and the generated caption text. Before captioning any frame batch, the pipeline checks whether a caption has already been produced for that segment, skipping previously completed items. This deduplication mechanism proved essential for resuming processing after infrastructure interruptions without redundant GPU computation.

The entire pipeline is containerized and can be executed locally via Docker or deployed at scale on Kubernetes using the provided Helm charts. The Kubernetes deployment provisions persistent storage for intermediate data and caches, avoiding redundant dataset downloads across restarts. Upon completion, caption records are aggregated and uploaded to the Hugging Face Hub as a unified, streamable dataset. The pipeline's modular design---with each stage operating independently with intermediate on-disk storage---enables components to be tuned and scaled independently and allows graceful recovery from partial failures. The total captioning workload consumed approximately 2.1 million GPU-hours of computation. All infrastructure code and configuration files are released alongside the dataset to enable reproducibility.

\section{Reasoning Strategies}
\label{sec:reasoning}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/reasoning-strategies.png}
    \caption{Illustration of reasoning strategy application for video caption generation. Five complementary strategies---Temporal Anchor Chain-of-Thought, Multi-Granularity Hierarchical Reasoning, Contrastive What-If Probing, Visual Evidence Citation, and Iterative Decomposition with Self-Verification---converge to produce a detailed final caption for a medical simulation training clip.}
    \label{fig:reasoning-strategies}
\end{figure}

The quality of video-language datasets depends not only on scale but critically on the accuracy and richness of textual descriptions paired with visual content. Our caption generation prompt incorporates five complementary reasoning strategies designed to ground descriptions in visual evidence: \textit{temporal anchor chain-of-thought}, which identifies key transition points and traces cause-effect relationships through the clip; \textit{multi-granularity hierarchical reasoning}, which processes the scene at global, mid-level, and fine-grained scales before reconciling these levels to detect inconsistencies; \textit{contrastive what-if probing}, which considers and rejects alternative interpretations based on observable evidence; \textit{visual evidence citation}, which requires referencing specific visual elements for each descriptive claim; and \textit{iterative decomposition with self-verification}, which creates a self-correcting loop through observation, action recognition, intent inference, summary, and verification steps. Together, these strategies encourage the captioning model to produce descriptions that capture spatial relationships, temporal progression, and semantic context rather than resorting to generic scene templates.

Figure~\ref{fig:reasoning-strategies} illustrates how these strategies converge on a representative clip from our dataset---a medical simulation training video from the ``Sim Week'' educational program. The temporal anchor approach traces the sequence from initial positioning through hands moving toward the patient to active intervention. Hierarchical reasoning processes the scene at the level of medical education context, team coordination phases, and individual hand positions and equipment placement. Contrastive probing considers whether the scene depicts actual surgery and grounds the correct interpretation (training simulation) in visual evidence such as the ``Sim Week'' overlay text and environmental context markers. The synthesis of these strategies produces our characteristic 117-word average captions---substantially longer than InternVid's 17.6 words~\cite{wang2024internvid} or MSR-VTT's 9.3 words~\cite{xu2016msrvtt}---while maintaining accuracy through multiple verification mechanisms that reduce hallucinated or unsupported claims.

\section{Dataset Analysis \& Characterization}
\label{sec:analysis}

This section presents a systematic characterization of The People's Video across three complementary dimensions: clip temporal structure, caption quality and vocabulary, and content domain coverage. Together, these analyses demonstrate that commercially licensed data can achieve the scale, diversity, and descriptive richness required by modern video-language models.

\subsection{Clip Duration Distribution}

Figure~\ref{fig:data-analysis} shows the distribution of clip durations across the 70.6M clips produced by our PySceneDetect-based segmentation pipeline. The distribution spans five temporal bins and reflects the natural scene-change cadence of our diverse source material rather than any post-hoc filtering.

\paragraph{Short clips (0--2s): 37\%.}
The largest single bin comprises clips of two seconds or fewer, arising predominantly from fast-cut technical presentations, performance sequences, and instructional demonstrations. Despite their brevity, these clips are valuable for action recognition, fine-grained motion understanding, and text-to-video generation evaluation, where model outputs are typically assessed at short temporal horizons.

\paragraph{Medium clips (2--10s): 45\%.}
The medium range, split approximately evenly between 2--4s (22\%) and 4--10s (23\%), constitutes the plurality of the dataset and aligns with the temporal horizon most commonly targeted by video captioning, video question answering, and temporal grounding benchmarks. The near-equal split between the two sub-ranges ensures that the dataset does not overfit to any single clip length within the medium tier.

\paragraph{Longer clips (10s+): 18\%.}
Clips exceeding ten seconds are divided between the 10--20s range (8\%) and clips longer than 20 seconds (10\%), providing extended temporal context for instructional video understanding, long-form video-language pre-training, and multi-step activity recognition. While proportionally smaller, the absolute size of this tier---roughly 12.7M clips---constitutes a substantial resource for tasks requiring sustained temporal reasoning.

The resulting average clip duration of 11.2 seconds is pulled upward by the long-tail distribution of clips exceeding 20 seconds, while the median duration falls in the medium range. This balance ensures computational efficiency during training while retaining sufficient temporal context for learning complex video-language relationships. Compared to HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m}, which averages 3.6 seconds per clip due to ASR-driven segmentation at subtitle boundaries, our content-aware segmentation produces clips that are substantially more temporally complete and semantically coherent.

\subsection{Caption Quality and Length}

Caption length is a necessary but not sufficient condition for descriptive richness: longer captions afford space to describe spatial relationships, temporal progression, inferred intent, and scene context in addition to salient objects and actions, though length alone does not guarantee quality. Table~\ref{tab:dataset_comparison} situates The People's Video captions within the landscape of existing datasets. At 117 words per clip on average, our captions are approximately $7\times$ longer than InternVid's 17.6-word average~\cite{wang2024internvid}, $13\times$ longer than MSR-VTT's 9.3-word average~\cite{xu2016msrvtt}, and roughly $4\times$ longer than HD-VILA-100M's 32.5-word ASR-derived descriptions~\cite{xue2022hdvila}. Among all large-scale datasets, only OpenVid-1M exceeds our average caption length (467 words)~\cite{nan2024openvid}, though its custom non-commercial license and order-of-magnitude smaller scale limit its utility for production deployments.

We therefore examine the lexical characteristics of the generated descriptions to verify that our captions' length reflects genuine descriptive depth. Analysis of the full caption corpus reveals that the 500 most frequent content words are dominated by three categories of vocabulary:

\paragraph{Visual and spatial descriptors.}
Terms such as \textit{background}, \textit{setting}, \textit{positioned}, \textit{visible}, and \textit{foreground} indicate that the captioning model consistently situates subjects within their physical environment, providing spatial context beyond simple object enumeration.

\paragraph{Human-centric attributes.}
High-frequency terms including \textit{person}, \textit{man}, \textit{wearing}, \textit{professional}, and \textit{seated} reflect the dataset's prevalence of instructional, educational, and presentational content featuring human subjects. The frequency of appearance-level descriptors such as \textit{wearing} and \textit{dressed} produces caption content useful for cross-modal retrieval and person-centric video understanding.

\paragraph{Inferential and epistemic language.}
Terms such as \textit{suggesting}, \textit{likely}, \textit{indicating}, \textit{possibly}, and \textit{overall} are among the most distinctive features of The People's Video vocabulary, as visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:word-cloud}. Rather than confining descriptions to directly observable facts, Qwen3-VL routinely provides interpretive commentary that bridges visual evidence and semantic meaning---for example, inferring from environmental context that a depicted procedure is a simulation rather than an actual surgical intervention. This inferential vocabulary is particularly well-suited to training multimodal large language models that must perform reasoning over visual content, not merely recognize it.

\subsection{Vocabulary Diversity and Corpus Statistics}

To characterize the breadth of the caption vocabulary, we computed word frequency distributions across the full corpus of 70.6M captions. The vocabulary exhibits a long-tailed frequency distribution characteristic of natural language corpora: a small set of function words and common visual descriptors occur with high frequency, while the long tail contains domain-specific and low-frequency descriptive terms that collectively provide the semantic richness distinguishing our captions from templated alternatives.

The word cloud in Figure~\ref{fig:word-cloud} confirms that high-frequency terms span multiple semantic roles---scene-level descriptors (\textit{background}, \textit{setting}), human-centric terms (\textit{person}, \textit{professional}), and epistemic markers (\textit{suggesting}, \textit{likely}, \textit{possibly})---rather than collapsing onto a narrow set of object labels. This vocabulary profile stands in contrast to datasets generated by simpler captioning pipelines, where high-frequency terms tend to be dominated by a small set of generic object categories. The diversity of caption vocabulary directly benefits video-language pre-training, where models learn cross-modal representations by aligning text tokens with visual features: a richer and more varied vocabulary creates a more discriminative training signal.

We additionally observe variation in caption length across content domains. Technical and professional content (software demonstrations, scientific lectures) produces the longest captions on average, reflecting the higher density of named entities, on-screen text references, and procedural detail in this domain. Everyday and environmental content (outdoor scenes, domestic activities) produces somewhat shorter captions, consistent with lower action density and fewer named referents. Educational and instructional content occupies an intermediate position, with captions frequently describing both the physical actions of instructors and the inferred purpose of demonstrated procedures.

\subsection{Content Domain Coverage}

The People's Video spans four broad domain clusters, each contributing distinct visual and linguistic characteristics to the corpus.

\paragraph{Technical and professional content.}
This cluster encompasses software demonstrations, engineering presentations, and scientific lectures. Clips in this domain frequently feature screen recordings, slides, and a single presenter addressing a camera or audience. The captions are distinguished by their references to on-screen text, named technologies, and the inferential framing of presenter intent---illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:data-sample} by the OpenStack Summit example, in which the caption identifies specific platform metrics displayed on slides and correctly attributes them to a product demonstration context.

\paragraph{Educational and instructional content.}
This cluster includes classroom instruction, how-to tutorials, skills training, and medical simulation footage such as the \textit{Sim Week} program shown in Figures~\ref{fig:sim-week} and~\ref{fig:reasoning-strategies}. This domain is particularly valuable for temporal reasoning tasks: instructional clips unfold as goal-directed action sequences with identifiable phases---setup, demonstration, and outcome---and the captions explicitly annotate these phases using temporal language (\textit{initially}, \textit{then}, \textit{transitioning to}). The medical simulation content is of particular note, representing a category underserved by general web-crawled datasets and directly relevant to healthcare AI applications.

\paragraph{Cultural and performative content.}
This cluster captures music, dance, theatre, and community events. Clips in this domain feature multiple subjects engaged in coordinated movement, as illustrated by the martial arts performance example in Figure~\ref{fig:data-sample}. Captions emphasize spatial configuration (\textit{positioned at the center}, \textit{arranged in rows}), relative motion (\textit{synchronized arm movements}, \textit{coordinated stances}), and audience context, providing the spatial and relational vocabulary needed for group activity recognition and multi-agent video understanding.

\paragraph{Everyday and environmental content.}
This cluster encompasses outdoor scenes, domestic activities, and community events, including atmospheric footage such as the misty road scene in Figure~\ref{fig:data-sample}. Captions in this domain make extensive use of environmental descriptors (\textit{foggy}, \textit{tree-lined}, \textit{dimly lit}) and epistemic hedging (\textit{suggests}, \textit{adding a subtle sense of}), reflecting the higher degree of interpretive ambiguity present in non-instructional scenes.

\subsection{Comparison with Existing Dataset Characteristics}

Taken together, the analyses above situate The People's Video in a previously unoccupied region of the dataset design space. Relative to datasets of comparable or greater scale---HowTo100M~\cite{miech2019howto100m}, InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid}, and Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m}---The People's Video offers substantially richer captions, higher resolution, and explicit per-video commercial licensing. Relative to datasets with permissive licensing---OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} and IACC.3~\cite{awad2022iacc3}---it provides an order-of-magnitude greater clip count and broader domain coverage. The clip duration distribution, averaging 11.2 seconds with meaningful mass at both short and long extremes, supports a wider range of downstream tasks than datasets with uniform or narrowly targeted clip lengths. The inferential vocabulary and multi-strategy caption design specifically address the requirements of video diffusion models and multimodal large language models that demand descriptions capturing temporal dynamics and semantic context, not merely static scene inventories.


\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate The People's Video across four dimensions: intrinsic dataset quality, downstream task performance, the effect of commercial license filtering on model capability, and the contribution of caption length to downstream performance. All experiments use the full 70.6M-clip dataset unless otherwise noted. Code and model checkpoints are released alongside the dataset to support reproducibility.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Intrinsic Dataset Quality}
\label{sec:exp-intrinsic}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Before evaluating downstream utility, we assess the quality of the dataset's two primary assets---its clips and its captions---through a combination of automatic metrics and human judgment.

\paragraph{Caption accuracy and hallucination rate.}
We assess caption factual accuracy by sampling 1,000 clips uniformly at random across the four domain clusters and collecting human annotations via a crowdsourced evaluation protocol. Annotators are shown three representative frames from each clip alongside its generated caption and asked to rate the caption on four dimensions: \textit{accuracy} (does the caption describe what is visually present?), \textit{completeness} (does it capture the main activity and context?), \textit{temporal coherence} (does it correctly reflect the temporal progression of the clip?), and \textit{hallucination} (does it assert facts not supported by the visual content?). Each clip is rated by three independent annotators; inter-annotator agreement is reported using Krippendorff's $\alpha$. To enable direct comparison, we apply the same protocol to random samples of equal size drawn from InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} and Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m}, providing a human-judged quality baseline for existing large-scale datasets. We additionally employ a vision-language model (Gemini 1.5 Pro) as an automatic hallucination judge: given the same frame--caption pairs, it is prompted to enumerate any claims in the caption that are inconsistent with the visual evidence. We report the fraction of captions containing at least one hallucinated claim, broken down by domain cluster.

\paragraph{Caption diversity.}
To verify that the elevated average caption length in The People's Video reflects genuine lexical richness rather than repetitive verbosity, we measure vocabulary diversity using two complementary statistics: the \textit{type-token ratio} (TTR), which measures the proportion of unique word types relative to total tokens, and \textit{MTLD} (Measure of Textual Lexical Diversity)~\cite{mccarthy2010mtld}, which is robust to corpus length differences that inflate naive TTR estimates. TTR is computed as the mean unique-token ratio over random groups of 100 captions, following the protocol of~\cite{mccarthy2010mtld}. MTLD is computed as the mean of the forward and backward length-normalized factor counts at the standard threshold of 0.72. We compare against InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} (streamed \texttt{InternVid-10M/FLT} split, \texttt{Caption} column) and OpenVid-1M~\cite{nan2024openvid} (streamed \texttt{train} split, \texttt{caption} column), each sampled at 10K captions to match the scale of the People's Video validation subset of 6{,}153 captions.

\paragraph{Caption diversity --- measured results.}
Table~\ref{tab:caption_diversity} reports the measured diversity statistics. The People's Video captions exhibit an MTLD of 98.6, compared to 68.5 for InternVid and 39.7 for OpenVid-1M, placing our corpus $1.4\times$ more lexically diverse than InternVid and $2.5\times$ more diverse than OpenVid-1M on this length-normalized measure. InternVid's comparatively high per-group TTR (0.443) is an artefact of its short average caption length (9.3 words on the measured sample): small pooled token counts per group produce mechanically higher unique-token ratios, a confound that MTLD's length-invariant construction specifically corrects. OpenVid-1M's low MTLD despite a caption length (104.3 words) comparable to ours (117.0 words) indicates that its longer captions are lexically repetitive, consistent with the templated behaviour typical of single-model captioning pipelines. These results provide direct empirical support for the claim that the elevated caption length of The People's Video reflects genuine descriptive depth rather than verbose repetition. We note that the caption lengths measured on the streamed comparator samples differ from the headline figures in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_comparison} (which quote each dataset's own reported averages); this likely reflects differences in subset, caption column, or tokenization, and is consistent with the broader observation that caption statistics are sensitive to the specific slice of a large dataset being evaluated.

\paragraph{Visual grounding quality.}
We measure the degree to which captions are visually grounded---rather than generic scene templates---using a CLIP-based alignment score. For each clip in a 50K-clip evaluation sample, we extract frame embeddings using CLIP ViT-L/14 and compute the cosine similarity between the mean frame embedding and the embedding of the corresponding caption. This score serves as an automatic proxy for visual grounding quality without requiring human annotation. We report the mean alignment score and its variance across domain clusters, and compare against the same metric computed for InternVid and Panda-70M captions paired with their respective source frames.

\paragraph{Clip segmentation quality.}
We evaluate the semantic quality of our PySceneDetect-based segmentation on a held-out set of 500 clips for which human annotators have independently marked ground-truth scene boundaries. We report boundary detection precision and recall at a one-second tolerance window. We additionally assess within-clip coherence: a vision-language model judge is shown the first and last frame of each clip and asked to rate whether both frames plausibly belong to the same semantic scene on a three-point scale. Clips that span an unintended scene transition---a segmentation failure mode---receive low coherence scores. We report the fraction of clips rated fully coherent and compare against a fixed-interval baseline that segments videos into uniform 10-second chunks, to quantify the benefit of content-aware boundary detection.

\subsection{Downstream Task Benchmarks}
\label{sec:exp-downstream}
% -------------------------------------------------------

We evaluate the utility of The People's Video for four canonical video-language tasks by fine-tuning standard architectures on our dataset and measuring performance on held-out benchmarks. In each case we compare against the same model trained on an equal number of clips sampled from InternVid~\cite{wang2024internvid} and Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m} to isolate the effect of dataset quality and licensing from model choice.

\paragraph{Text-to-video retrieval.}
Text-to-video retrieval requires a model to rank a corpus of videos by relevance to a natural language query. We fine-tune CLIP4Clip~\cite{luo2022clip4clip} with a mean-pooling temporal aggregator on People's Video clip--caption pairs and evaluate on three standard benchmarks: MSR-VTT~\cite{xu2016msrvtt} (1K test split), DiDeMo~\cite{hendricks2017didemo}, and ActivityNet Captions~\cite{krishna2017anetcaption}. We report Recall at rank 1, 5, and 10 (R@1, R@5, R@10) and median rank (MdR) for both text-to-video and video-to-text retrieval directions. To assess zero-shot transfer capability---important for a dataset intended as a general-purpose pre-training source---we also evaluate a version of the model without any benchmark-specific fine-tuning.

\paragraph{Video captioning.}
Video captioning requires a model to generate a natural language description of a video clip. We fine-tune Video-LLaVA~\cite{lin2023videollava} on People's Video clip--caption pairs and evaluate on MSR-VTT, YouCook2~\cite{zhou2018youcook2}, and ActivityNet Captions. We report CIDEr, METEOR, and ROUGE-L on each benchmark. Because our captions are substantially longer than the ground-truth references in these benchmarks (which were collected via manual annotation of short descriptions), we additionally evaluate on a held-out subset of People's Video itself using captions withheld from training, to provide a benchmark where caption length and style are matched to the training distribution.

\paragraph{Video question answering.}
Video question answering (VideoQA) tests a model's ability to answer natural language questions about video content. We fine-tune a VideoQA model on People's Video and evaluate on NExT-QA~\cite{xiao2021nextqa}, MSVD-QA~\cite{xu2017msvdqa}, and ActivityNet-QA~\cite{yu2019activitynetqa}. NExT-QA is of particular interest because it categorises questions by reasoning type---causal, temporal, and descriptive---allowing us to separately assess whether the inferential vocabulary of People's Video captions provides measurable gains on questions requiring intent and causality reasoning beyond simple perceptual recognition.

\paragraph{Temporal action localization.}
Temporal action localization requires a model to identify the start and end times of specific activities within an untrimmed video. We train a temporal grounding model on the long-clip subset of People's Video (clips exceeding 10 seconds, approximately 12.7M clips) and evaluate on Charades-STA~\cite{gao2017charades} and the temporal localization split of ActivityNet Captions. We report Recall at IoU thresholds of 0.3, 0.5, and 0.7 (R@\{0.3, 0.5, 0.7\}). This experiment specifically assesses the value of the 18\% long-clip tier of the dataset, which was designed to support exactly this class of tasks.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Effect of Commercial License Filtering}
\label{sec:exp-licensing}
% -------------------------------------------------------

A central empirical question for this paper is whether restricting training data to commercially licensed content meaningfully degrades model capability relative to unrestricted corpora of equivalent scale. We address this through a matched retrieval comparison and a content coverage analysis.

\paragraph{Matched retrieval comparison.}
To isolate the effect of license filtering from confounds such as domain, resolution, and clip length, we construct a controlled baseline by sampling an equal number of clips from Panda-70M~\cite{chen2024panda70m} matched to the People's Video distribution across domain labels, clip duration bins, and source video resolution. We train the same CLIP4Clip retrieval model from Section~\ref{sec:exp-downstream} on both the People's Video clips and the matched Panda-70M sample, holding all hyperparameters fixed. Retrieval performance on MSR-VTT and DiDeMo is reported for both conditions. A small or negligible performance gap would provide direct empirical evidence that commercially licensed data can match the utility of unrestricted corpora, while a large gap would motivate further curation effort to close coverage deficiencies.

\paragraph{Activity category coverage gap.}
To quantify which activity categories are underrepresented in the CC-licensed subset relative to uncurated web video, we map clips from both People's Video and the matched Panda-70M sample to the 203-class ActivityNet taxonomy~\cite{heilbron2015activitynet} using a zero-shot video classifier. We compute the frequency distribution of activity categories for each corpus and report the Gini coefficient of category coverage as a summary measure of distributional uniformity. We additionally rank categories by their coverage gap (Panda-70M frequency minus People's Video frequency) to identify the specific activity types most underrepresented in commercially licensed content, informing targeted future curation efforts.

\paragraph{Demographic and geographic diversity.}
We characterise the demographic and geographic properties of People's Video relative to InternVid as a representative uncurated baseline. On a stratified 10K-clip sample from each dataset, we apply a face attribute classifier to estimate the apparent gender and age distribution of on-screen subjects across domain clusters, reporting distributions rather than individual-level labels. We measure linguistic diversity as the fraction of clips containing non-English speech, detected using a language identification model applied to the audio track. Geographic diversity is assessed using the fraction of videos with geotag metadata outside North America and Western Europe. These measurements are intended to characterise distributional properties of the dataset and to identify axes along which future curation should prioritise expansion.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Caption Length Ablation}
\label{sec:exp-caption-length}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Our captions average 117 words per clip, substantially exceeding those of any comparable large-scale dataset. To isolate the contribution of caption length to downstream model performance---and to provide practical guidance for future dataset efforts---we conduct a controlled ablation over caption length while holding all other dataset and training variables fixed.

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
We construct three caption variants from an identical 10M-clip subset of People's Video by truncating the full generated captions to different target lengths: \textit{(a) full captions} at the natural average length of 117 words; \textit{(b) 32-word truncation}, matching the average length of HD-VILA-100M's ASR-derived captions~\cite{xue2022hdvila}; and \textit{(c) 17-word truncation}, matching InternVid's average caption length~\cite{wang2024internvid}. Truncation is applied at sentence boundaries to preserve grammatical completeness. A CLIP4Clip retrieval model and a Video-LLaVA captioning model are trained independently on each variant, with all hyperparameters held fixed across conditions. This design ensures that any performance differences are attributable solely to caption length rather than to clip selection, model architecture, or training procedure.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
We evaluate each condition on MSR-VTT and DiDeMo retrieval (R@1) and on YouCook2 captioning (CIDEr). Performance is plotted as a function of caption length across the three conditions to visualise the marginal return on descriptive depth. We additionally report CLIP alignment scores (Section~\ref{sec:exp-intrinsic}) for each caption variant, separating the question of whether longer captions improve downstream task performance from the separate question of whether they improve visual grounding quality as measured intrinsically.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Licensing scope and legal limitations.}
While every video in The People's Video carries a creator-declared Creative Commons license (CC0 or CC-BY) as recorded in YouTube's metadata API, this per-video license governs the rights granted by the \emph{creator} over their content and does not, on its own, resolve all legal dimensions of data acquisition. In particular, YouTube's Terms of Service impose platform-level restrictions on downloading and redistribution that operate independently of the content license chosen by individual creators. Whether a Creative Commons declaration by a creator constitutes sufficient authorization to download and redistribute that content outside of YouTube---or whether YouTube's platform-level ToS imposes an additional, overriding constraint---remains an open legal question that has not been adjudicated in any jurisdiction to our knowledge. We do not claim that the CC license tags fully immunize downstream users from platform-level legal risk, and practitioners deploying models trained on this data in commercial settings should seek independent legal counsel to assess jurisdictional exposure.

\paragraph{License stability and verification limits.}
A further limitation is that Creative Commons license declarations on YouTube are mutable: a creator may change a video's license from CC-BY to the standard YouTube license at any time after publication, retroactively narrowing the permissions that existed at the time of collection. Our pipeline records the license tag at the point of acquisition, but we do not currently implement continuous license monitoring to detect such changes post-collection. As a result, the licensing status of individual videos in the dataset may diverge from their current YouTube metadata over time. While the legal implications of retroactive license changes for content already downloaded under a permissive license are unsettled, this mutability introduces a practical ambiguity that users should be aware of. Future versions of the dataset will incorporate automated license re-verification to flag or remove videos whose declared license has changed, strengthening the long-term legal integrity of the collection.

\paragraph{Frame sampling and temporal coverage.}
Our frame extraction pipeline samples frames at 30-second intervals from source videos (Section~\ref{sec:frame-extraction}). Because the average clip duration is 11.2 seconds and 37\% of clips are under 2 seconds, most clips are captioned from one or two frames rather than dense temporal samples. This coarse sampling limits the captioning model's ability to describe fine-grained temporal dynamics, particularly for short clips, and means that claims of temporal progression in captions are best understood as inferred from sparse visual snapshots rather than observed from continuous video. Future work could explore denser frame sampling strategies to improve temporal caption fidelity, though at increased computational cost.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced The People's Video, a large-scale multimodal dataset of 847K videos, 70.6M clips, and 219K hours of content, distinguished from all prior work by its combination of commercially permissive CC0 and CC-BY licensing, 720P resolution, and state-of-the-art caption quality averaging 117 words per clip. By curating exclusively from the pool of YouTube videos whose creators have explicitly elected Creative Commons licensing, and by applying a diversity-aware curation strategy across four broad domain clusters, we demonstrate that the perceived trade-off between licensing compliance and dataset scale is not fundamental---it is an artifact of insufficient attention to the structure of existing permissive content on major platforms.

The dataset addresses two converging pressures facing the video AI community. The first is legal: the ongoing wave of copyright litigation surrounding AI training data has made licensing provenance a first-class concern for both academic and commercial practitioners, yet virtually every large-scale video dataset in wide use today either draws indiscriminately from YouTube in violation of its terms of service or imposes non-commercial restrictions that preclude production deployment. The People's Video provides documentary licensing evidence at the per-video level, offering the auditability that legal and compliance teams increasingly require, though residual legal questions regarding the interaction between per-video CC licenses and YouTube's platform-level Terms of Service remain (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}). The second pressure is technical: modern video diffusion models and multimodal large language models demand training captions that capture scene dynamics, spatial context, and semantic context, not merely object inventories. Our Qwen3-VL-based captioning pipeline, guided by five complementary reasoning strategies and deployed at scale through ScalarLM's distributed inference infrastructure, produces descriptions that meet this bar while processing over 70.6 million clips across approximately 2.1 million GPU-hours of computation.

\paragraph{Limitations.} Several limitations of the current work merit acknowledgment. First, while CC0 and CC-BY licenses are commercially permissive, they do not resolve all legal questions that may arise in specific jurisdictions or deployment contexts; practitioners should seek independent legal counsel for high-stakes commercial applications. Second, despite our diversity-aware curation process, the dataset inevitably reflects the demographics and topics of creators who choose to publish on YouTube under open licenses, which may skew toward certain geographic regions, languages, and production styles. Systematic measurement and mitigation of these distributional biases is an important direction for future work. Third, automated caption generation at this scale, even with verification-oriented reasoning strategies, introduces some rate of hallucinated or inaccurate descriptions; we report caption quality metrics in Section~\ref{sec:analysis} but acknowledge that a fully human-verified caption subset would provide a stronger quality upper bound. Fourth, our 30-second frame sampling interval means that most clips are captioned from sparse visual snapshots, limiting the temporal fidelity of descriptions particularly for short clips. Finally, the dataset's coverage of long-form video (clips exceeding 20 seconds) remains proportionally small at 10\% of the collection, which may limit its utility for tasks requiring extended temporal reasoning without supplementation from other sources.

\paragraph{Future Directions.} Several extensions of this work are particularly promising. Expanding the dataset through continued curation---targeting underrepresented geographic regions, non-English language content, and long-tail activity categories---would further close the diversity gap relative to unconstrained web-crawled corpora. Incorporating automatic license monitoring to flag videos whose stated license changes after collection would strengthen the dataset's long-term legal integrity. On the technical side, applying emerging diffusion-based captioning approaches~\cite{ref:dot-neurips2024, ref:dimple-arxiv2025} as they mature could improve both caption accuracy and generation efficiency at scale. Denser frame sampling strategies could improve the temporal fidelity of captions, particularly for short clips. We also anticipate that The People's Video will enable systematic study of how licensing-aware data curation affects model behavior: whether commercially licensed data, despite being a filtered subset of the broader web, produces models with meaningfully different capability or bias profiles compared to models trained on unrestricted corpora is an open empirical question of both scientific and societal importance.

The People's Video dataset, curation tools, captioning infrastructure, and all associated code are publicly released to the research community. We hope this work lowers the barrier for researchers and practitioners who require both scale and legal clarity, and encourages the broader community to treat licensing as an integral dimension of dataset design rather than an afterthought.
